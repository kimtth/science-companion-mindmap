### 1. Cell Biology
The study of cells, the basic unit of life. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells:
- **Cell Structure**: Organization of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells
  - **Prokaryotes** (bacteria, archaea): No membrane-bound nucleus, smaller (1-10 Œºm)
    - Nucleoid region contains circular DNA
    - Ribosomes (70S), cell wall, plasma membrane
    - Some have flagella, pili, capsule
  - **Eukaryotes** (protists, fungi, plants, animals): Membrane-bound organelles, larger (10-100 Œºm)
    - **Nucleus**: DNA organized in chromosomes, nuclear envelope with pores
    - **Mitochondria**: Cellular respiration, ATP production, own DNA (maternal inheritance)
    - **Endoplasmic reticulum**: Rough (ribosomes, protein synthesis) vs Smooth (lipid synthesis)
    - **Golgi apparatus**: Protein modification, sorting, packaging, secretion
    - **Lysosomes**: Hydrolytic enzymes for digestion (pH ‚âà 5)
    - **Peroxisomes**: Oxidative reactions, break down fatty acids
    - **Chloroplasts** (plants): Photosynthesis, own DNA
    - **Vacuoles**: Storage (large central vacuole in plants for turgor)
    - **Cytoskeleton**: Microfilaments (actin), intermediate filaments, microtubules (tubulin)
      - Functions: shape, movement, transport, division
- **Membrane Structure & Transport**: Cell boundaries and material exchange
  - **Fluid mosaic model**: Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins
  - Amphipathic phospholipids: hydrophilic heads out, hydrophobic tails in
  - Membrane proteins: integral (transmembrane) vs peripheral (surface)
  - Cholesterol: modulates fluidity in animal cells
  - **Passive transport**: No ATP required, down concentration gradient
    - Simple diffusion: small nonpolar (O‚ÇÇ, CO‚ÇÇ)
    - Facilitated diffusion: channel proteins (ions) or carrier proteins (glucose)
    - Osmosis: water diffusion through aquaporins or lipid bilayer
  - **Active transport**: Requires ATP, against concentration gradient
    - Primary: ATP directly used (Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫-ATPase pump: 3 Na‚Å∫ out, 2 K‚Å∫ in)
    - Secondary: Uses ion gradient (symport, antiport)
  - **Bulk transport**: Large molecules/particles
    - Endocytosis: phagocytosis (solids), pinocytosis (liquids), receptor-mediated
    - Exocytosis: vesicles fuse with membrane to release contents
  - **Tonicity**: Effect of external solution on cell
    - Isotonic: no net water movement
    - Hypertonic: water leaves cell (crenation in animals, plasmolysis in plants)
    - Hypotonic: water enters cell (lysis in animals, turgor in plants)
- **Cell Metabolism**: Energy production and utilization
  - **Cellular respiration**: Glucose + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO + ATP
    - **Glycolysis** (cytoplasm): Glucose ‚Üí 2 pyruvate; Net: 2 ATP, 2 NADH
      - Investment phase (uses 2 ATP) and payoff phase (produces 4 ATP)
    - **Pyruvate oxidation** (mitochondrial matrix): 2 pyruvate ‚Üí 2 acetyl-CoA + 2 CO‚ÇÇ; 2 NADH
    - **Krebs cycle** (citric acid cycle, matrix): Per glucose: 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH‚ÇÇ, 4 CO‚ÇÇ
    - **Electron transport chain** (inner mitochondrial membrane): ~32-34 ATP via oxidative phosphorylation
      - NADH ‚Üí Complex I, FADH‚ÇÇ ‚Üí Complex II
      - Proton gradient (chemiosmosis): H‚Å∫ pumped to intermembrane space
      - ATP synthase: H‚Å∫ flows back, drives ATP synthesis
      - O‚ÇÇ is final electron acceptor ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO
    - **Total**: ~36-38 ATP per glucose (theoretical; actual ‚âà 30-32)
  - **Photosynthesis**: CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO + light ‚Üí Glucose + O‚ÇÇ
    - **Light reactions** (thylakoid membrane): Light ‚Üí ATP + NADPH + O‚ÇÇ
      - Photosystem II: splits H‚ÇÇO ‚Üí O‚ÇÇ + 4H‚Å∫ + 4e‚Åª
      - Electron transport chain: pumps H‚Å∫ into thylakoid space
      - Photosystem I: re-energizes electrons
      - NADP‚Å∫ reductase: produces NADPH
      - ATP synthase: chemiosmosis produces ATP
      - Cyclic vs non-cyclic photophosphorylation
    - **Calvin cycle** (stroma): CO‚ÇÇ fixation ‚Üí G3P ‚Üí glucose
      - Carbon fixation: Rubisco adds CO‚ÇÇ to RuBP ‚Üí 2 molecules of 3-PGA
      - Reduction: 3-PGA ‚Üí G3P (uses ATP and NADPH)
      - Regeneration: G3P ‚Üí RuBP (uses ATP)
      - 3 CO‚ÇÇ + 9 ATP + 6 NADPH ‚Üí 1 G3P
      - 6 turns to make 1 glucose
  - **Fermentation**: Anaerobic ATP production
    - Glycolysis + regeneration of NAD‚Å∫
    - Alcohol fermentation: pyruvate ‚Üí ethanol + CO‚ÇÇ (yeast)
    - Lactic acid fermentation: pyruvate ‚Üí lactate (muscles, bacteria)
    - Only 2 ATP per glucose (from glycolysis)
- **Cell Division**: Growth and reproduction
  - **Cell cycle**: G‚ÇÅ (growth) ‚Üí S (DNA replication) ‚Üí G‚ÇÇ (growth) ‚Üí M (mitosis) ‚Üí cytokinesis
  - Checkpoints: G‚ÇÅ/S, G‚ÇÇ/M, metaphase (spindle checkpoint)
  - Cyclins and CDKs regulate progression
  - **Mitosis**: Produces 2 identical diploid cells
    - Prophase: chromatin condenses, spindle forms, nuclear envelope breaks
    - Metaphase: chromosomes align at metaphase plate
    - Anaphase: sister chromatids separate to opposite poles
    - Telophase: nuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense
    - Cytokinesis: cleavage furrow (animals) or cell plate (plants)
  - **Meiosis**: Produces 4 non-identical haploid gametes
    - Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes separate (reductional division)
      - Prophase I: crossing over (recombination) during synapsis
      - Metaphase I: independent assortment of homologs
    - Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate (like mitosis)
    - Sources of variation: crossing over, independent assortment, random fertilization
- **Cell Communication**: Signal transduction pathways
  - Reception: Signal molecule (ligand) binds receptor
  - Transduction: Signal amplified through cascade
  - Response: Cellular activity altered
  - Types: endocrine (hormones), paracrine (local), autocrine (self), juxtacrine (contact)
  - Second messengers: cAMP, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, IP‚ÇÉ, DAG
  - Protein kinase cascades amplify signal

**Cellular Principles**:
- Cell theory: all organisms made of cells, cells from preexisting cells, cells are basic unit
- Surface area to volume ratio limits cell size
- Compartmentalization allows specialized functions
- Energy coupling: exergonic drives endergonic (ATP)
- Chemiosmosis: universal mechanism for ATP synthesis

**Key Applications**: Medicine, biotechnology, cancer research, drug development, regenerative medicine, synthetic biology

### 2. Genetics
The science of heredity and genetic variation - information flow from DNA to traits:
- **DNA Structure**: Double helix and genetic information storage
  - Watson-Crick model: antiparallel double helix
  - Base pairing: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds)
  - Chargaff's rules: %A = %T, %G = %C
  - Major and minor grooves (protein binding sites)
  - Chromosomes: DNA + histones (nucleosomes) ‚Üí chromatin
  - Euchromatin (loose, active) vs heterochromatin (condensed, inactive)
- **DNA Replication**: Semiconservative, each strand serves as template
  - Origin of replication: replication bubble forms
  - **Helicase**: unwinds double helix
  - **Single-strand binding proteins**: stabilize unwound DNA
  - **Primase**: synthesizes RNA primer
  - **DNA polymerase III**: adds nucleotides 5'‚Üí3' (main enzyme)
    - Leading strand: continuous synthesis
    - Lagging strand: discontinuous (Okazaki fragments)
  - **DNA polymerase I**: removes RNA primers, fills gaps
  - **Ligase**: joins Okazaki fragments
  - **Topoisomerase**: relieves tension from unwinding
  - Proofreading: 3'‚Üí5' exonuclease activity
  - Error rate: ~1 in 10¬π‚Å∞ nucleotides (very accurate)
- **Gene Expression**: From DNA to RNA to proteins (Central Dogma)
  - **Transcription**: DNA ‚Üí RNA
    - **Initiation**: RNA polymerase binds promoter (TATA box in eukaryotes)
    - **Elongation**: RNA polymerase synthesizes mRNA 5'‚Üí3'
    - **Termination**: Rho-independent or Rho-dependent (prokaryotes)
    - Template strand read 3'‚Üí5', mRNA synthesized 5'‚Üí3'
    - In eukaryotes: RNA polymerase II transcribes mRNA
  - **RNA Processing** (eukaryotes only):
    - 5' cap (7-methylguanosine): protects, aids translation
    - 3' poly-A tail: protects, aids translation
    - **Splicing**: introns removed, exons joined
      - Spliceosome (snRNPs) catalyzes
      - Alternative splicing: different combinations of exons
        - Increases protein diversity from single gene
  - **Translation**: mRNA ‚Üí protein at ribosomes
    - Genetic code: 64 codons, 20 amino acids
    - Start codon: AUG (methionine)
    - Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (no tRNA)
    - Degeneracy: multiple codons per amino acid (wobble base pairing)
    - **Initiation**: ribosome assembles at start codon, initiator tRNA (met)
    - **Elongation**: tRNA delivers amino acids, peptide bond forms
      - A site (aminoacyl): incoming tRNA
      - P site (peptidyl): tRNA with growing chain
      - E site (exit): empty tRNA leaves
    - **Termination**: release factor recognizes stop codon
    - Direction: mRNA read 5'‚Üí3', protein synthesized N‚ÜíC terminus
  - Post-translational modifications: folding, cleavage, chemical modifications
- **Gene Regulation**: Controlling gene expression
  - **Prokaryotes**: Operons (cluster of genes under single promoter)
    - **lac operon**: negative control (repressor blocks in absence of lactose)
      - Inducer (allolactose) inactivates repressor
    - **trp operon**: negative control (repressor blocks in presence of tryptophan)
      - Corepressor (tryptophan) activates repressor
    - Positive control: CAP-cAMP activates transcription
  - **Eukaryotes**: Multiple levels of regulation
    - **Chromatin structure**: histone acetylation (loosens, activates), methylation (varies)
    - **Transcriptional**: transcription factors, enhancers, silencers
      - Enhancers: far from promoter, increase transcription
      - Transcription factors bind promoter/enhancer
    - **Post-transcriptional**: alternative splicing, mRNA stability
    - **Translational**: regulatory proteins, miRNA
    - **Post-translational**: protein modification, degradation
  - **Epigenetics**: Heritable changes without DNA sequence change
    - DNA methylation (cytosine): gene silencing
    - Histone modifications: acetylation, methylation, phosphorylation
    - X-inactivation in females (Barr body)
    - Genomic imprinting: parent-of-origin expression
- **Mendelian Inheritance**: Patterns of trait transmission
  - **Law of Segregation**: Allele pairs separate during gamete formation
  - **Law of Independent Assortment**: Different genes assort independently (if unlinked)
  - Dominant alleles expressed in heterozygotes
  - Recessive alleles expressed only in homozygotes
  - Punnett squares predict offspring ratios
  - Monohybrid cross: 3:1 phenotypic ratio
  - Dihybrid cross: 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio (if independent)
  - Test cross: cross with homozygous recessive to determine genotype
- **Extensions of Mendelian Genetics**:
  - **Incomplete dominance**: Heterozygote intermediate phenotype
  - **Codominance**: Both alleles fully expressed (ABO blood type)
  - **Multiple alleles**: >2 alleles in population (ABO: I·¥¨, I·¥Æ, i)
  - **Pleiotropy**: One gene affects multiple traits (sickle cell)
  - **Epistasis**: One gene masks another
  - **Polygenic inheritance**: Multiple genes affect one trait (height, skin color)
    - Continuous variation, bell-shaped distribution
  - **Environmental effects**: Phenotype = genotype + environment
  - **Linked genes**: On same chromosome, don't assort independently
    - Recombination frequency ‚àù distance between genes
    - Gene mapping: recombination frequencies determine order
- **Mutations**: Changes in DNA sequence
  - **Point mutations**: Single nucleotide changes
    - Silent: no amino acid change (wobble)
    - Missense: different amino acid (sickle cell: Glu‚ÜíVal)
    - Nonsense: premature stop codon
  - **Frameshift**: Insertion/deletion not multiple of 3
    - Shifts reading frame, usually catastrophic
  - **Chromosomal mutations**: Large-scale changes
    - Deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation
  - Causes: errors in replication, mutagens (chemicals, radiation)
  - Some beneficial (source of variation), most neutral or harmful
- **Biotechnology**: Applied genetics
  - **Recombinant DNA**: Combining DNA from different sources
    - Restriction enzymes: cut DNA at specific sequences
    - Plasmids: bacterial vectors for gene transfer
    - DNA ligase: joins DNA fragments
  - **PCR** (Polymerase Chain Reaction): Amplify DNA
    - Denature (95¬∞C) ‚Üí Anneal primers (55¬∞C) ‚Üí Extend (72¬∞C)
    - Exponential amplification: 2‚Åø copies after n cycles
  - **Gel electrophoresis**: Separate DNA by size
  - **DNA sequencing**: Determine nucleotide sequence
  - **CRISPR-Cas9**: Precise genome editing
  - **Cloning**: Produce genetically identical organisms
  - **GMOs**: Genetically modified organisms (Bt corn, golden rice)
  - **Gene therapy**: Treat disease by modifying genes

**Genetics Principles**:
- One gene-one polypeptide hypothesis (updated from one gene-one enzyme)
- Central dogma: DNA ‚Üí RNA ‚Üí protein (with exceptions: reverse transcription)
- Genetic code is universal (nearly all organisms)
- Information flow is highly regulated at multiple levels
- Mutations are ultimate source of genetic variation

**Conceptual Insights**:
- Gene expression is tightly regulated (not all genes active in all cells)
- Alternative splicing allows one gene ‚Üí multiple proteins
- Epigenetics shows environment can affect gene expression without changing DNA
- Most DNA is non-coding (regulatory, structural, or unknown function)
- Mendelian genetics applies to discrete traits; most traits are polygenic

**Key Applications**: Genetic counseling, gene therapy, GMO development, forensics, personalized medicine, agriculture, evolutionary studies

### 3. Evolution
The unifying principle of biology - descent with modification. Evolution explains both unity and diversity of life:
- **Natural Selection**: Darwin's mechanism of adaptive evolution
  - **Observations**:
    1. Overproduction: More offspring than can survive
    2. Variation: Individuals differ in traits
    3. Heritability: Traits passed to offspring
  - **Inferences**:
    1. Differential survival and reproduction (fitness)
    2. Accumulation of favorable traits over generations
  - Result: Population evolves (allele frequencies change)
  - Individuals don't evolve; populations evolve
  - Natural selection acts on phenotypes, but evolution is change in alleles
- **Types of Natural Selection**:
  - **Directional**: Favors one extreme (giraffes' necks, antibiotic resistance)
  - **Stabilizing**: Favors intermediate (human birth weight)
  - **Disruptive**: Favors both extremes (beak sizes in variable food supply)
  - **Sexual selection**: Traits increase mating success (peacock tails)
    - Intrasexual: competition within sex
    - Intersexual: mate choice
  - **Balancing selection**: Maintains variation (heterozygote advantage)
- **Evidence for Evolution**: Multiple independent lines of evidence
  1. **Fossil record**: Documents history of life
     - Transitional forms (Tiktaalik: fish ‚Üí tetrapod)
     - Succession of forms over time
     - Mass extinctions and radiations
  2. **Comparative anatomy**:
     - **Homologous structures**: Same origin, different function (mammalian forelimbs)
       - Evidence of common ancestry
     - **Analogous structures**: Different origin, same function (bird/insect wings)
       - Evidence of convergent evolution
     - **Vestigial structures**: Reduced/non-functional remnants (human appendix, whale pelvis)
  3. **Comparative embryology**: Similar developmental stages (pharyngeal pouches)
  4. **Molecular biology**: DNA/protein similarities
     - More similar sequences = more recent common ancestor
     - Molecular clocks: neutral mutations accumulate at constant rate
     - Pseudogenes and shared mistakes
  5. **Biogeography**: Species distribution patterns
     - Island species similar to mainland (Gal√°pagos finches)
     - Continental drift explains distributions
  6. **Direct observation**: Evolution in action
     - Antibiotic resistance in bacteria
     - Peppered moth color shift
     - HIV evolution in patients
     - Experimental evolution in laboratories
- **Speciation**: Formation of new species
  - **Species concept**: Biological species can interbreed and produce fertile offspring
    - Other concepts: morphological, ecological, phylogenetic
  - **Reproductive isolation**: Barriers to gene flow
    - **Prezygotic** (before fertilization):
      - Habitat isolation, temporal isolation, behavioral isolation
      - Mechanical isolation, gametic isolation
    - **Postzygotic** (after fertilization):
      - Reduced hybrid viability, reduced hybrid fertility, hybrid breakdown
  - **Allopatric speciation**: Geographic isolation
    - Population separated by barrier
    - Different selection pressures or genetic drift
    - Most common mode
  - **Sympatric speciation**: No geographic barrier
    - Polyploidy (especially in plants): chromosome doubling
    - Sexual selection, habitat specialization
  - Rate: gradualism (slow, steady) vs punctuated equilibrium (rapid bursts)
- **Population Genetics**: Allele frequencies in populations
  - **Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium**: No evolution baseline
    - Allele frequencies: p + q = 1
    - Genotype frequencies: p¬≤ + 2pq + q¬≤ = 1
    - **Conditions** (if met, no evolution):
      1. No mutations
      2. Random mating
      3. No natural selection
      4. No gene flow (migration)
      5. Large population (no genetic drift)
    - Use: detect if evolution occurring by comparing observed to expected
  - **Genetic drift**: Random changes in allele frequencies
    - **Bottleneck effect**: Population size drastically reduced
    - **Founder effect**: Small group colonizes new area
    - Stronger effect in small populations
    - Can fix deleterious alleles or lose beneficial alleles
  - **Gene flow**: Allele movement between populations (migration)
    - Homogenizes populations, opposes speciation
    - Introduces new alleles
  - **Mutation**: Ultimate source of new alleles
    - Provides raw material for evolution
    - Usually neutral or deleterious, rarely beneficial
  - **Non-random mating**: Affects genotype (not allele) frequencies
    - Inbreeding: increases homozygosity
    - Sexual selection: non-random mate choice
- **Macroevolution**: Large-scale evolutionary changes
  - Origin of new taxonomic groups
  - Evolutionary trends
  - **Adaptive radiation**: Rapid diversification (mammals after dinosaurs)
  - **Convergent evolution**: Similar environments ‚Üí similar traits
  - **Coevolution**: Reciprocal evolutionary changes (flowers and pollinators)
  - **Extinction**: 99% of species that ever lived are extinct

**Evolutionary Principles**:
- Evolution is change in allele frequencies over time
- Natural selection is non-random survival and reproduction
- Evolution is not goal-directed or progressive
- Organisms don't evolve during their lifetime (acquired traits not inherited)
- Evolution is both fact (descent with modification occurred) and theory (mechanisms)

**Conceptual Insights**:
- Fitness is reproductive success, not strength
- Adaptations are traits that increase fitness in specific environment
- Evolution can only modify existing structures (constraints)
- Trade-offs: no perfect organism (pleiotropy, genetic correlations)
- Common ancestry explains both similarities and differences among species
- Microevolution (within species) + time = macroevolution (new species)

**Key Applications**: Medicine (antibiotic resistance, vaccine design), agriculture (crop improvement, pest management), conservation (preserving genetic diversity), understanding disease origins

### 4. Ecology
Interactions between organisms and their environment - study of distribution and abundance. Ecology operates at multiple levels:
- **Levels of Organization**: Individual ‚Üí Population ‚Üí Community ‚Üí Ecosystem ‚Üí Biosphere
- **Population Ecology**: Population dynamics and growth
  - **Population**: Same species, same area, same time
  - **Density**: Number per unit area or volume
  - **Dispersion**: Clumped (most common), uniform, random
  - **Population growth models**:
    - **Exponential growth**: dN/dt = r_max √ó N (J-shaped curve)
      - Unlimited resources, no limits
      - r_max = intrinsic rate of increase (b - d)
      - Doubling time: t_d = 0.693/r
    - **Logistic growth**: dN/dt = r_max √ó N √ó (K-N)/K (S-shaped curve)
      - K = carrying capacity (maximum sustainable population)
      - Growth slows as N approaches K
      - More realistic than exponential
  - **Limiting factors**:
    - **Density-dependent**: Competition, predation, disease, stress
      - Effect increases with population density
    - **Density-independent**: Weather, natural disasters, climate
      - Effect independent of population density
  - **Life history strategies**:
    - **r-selected**: Many offspring, little parental care, early reproduction, short lifespan
      - Opportunists in unstable environments (insects, weeds)
    - **K-selected**: Few offspring, extensive parental care, late reproduction, long lifespan
      - Stable environments near carrying capacity (elephants, humans)
    - Most species intermediate
  - **Survivorship curves**:
    - Type I: Low mortality until old age (humans, large mammals)
    - Type II: Constant mortality rate (birds, rodents)
    - Type III: High early mortality (fish, plants, invertebrates)
- **Community Ecology**: Species interactions
  - **Competition** (-/-): Both species harmed
    - **Interspecific**: Between different species
    - **Intraspecific**: Within same species (usually stronger)
    - **Competitive exclusion**: Complete competitors can't coexist
    - **Resource partitioning**: Niche differentiation reduces competition
    - **Character displacement**: Evolution reduces competition
  - **Predation** (+/-): Consumer benefits, prey harmed
    - **Predator-prey cycles**: Coupled oscillations (lynx-hare)
    - **Coevolution**: Arms race (predator adaptations ‚Üî prey defenses)
    - **Prey defenses**: Camouflage, warning coloration, mimicry, chemical defense
  - **Herbivory** (+/-): Like predation but eating plants
    - Plant defenses: thorns, toxins, tough leaves
  - **Symbiosis**: Close, long-term interactions
    - **Mutualism** (+/+): Both benefit (bees and flowers, mycorrhizae)
      - Obligate (required) vs facultative (optional)
    - **Commensalism** (+/0): One benefits, other unaffected (barnacles on whales)
    - **Parasitism** (+/-): Parasite benefits, host harmed
      - Endoparasites (inside) vs ectoparasites (outside)
  - **Keystone species**: Disproportionate impact on community
    - Removal causes major changes (sea otter ‚Üî sea urchins ‚Üî kelp)
  - **Community structure**:
    - Species richness: number of species
    - Relative abundance: evenness of populations
    - Diversity indices combine richness and evenness
  - **Succession**: Predictable changes in community over time
    - **Primary**: Starts from bare substrate (lava, glacial retreat)
      - Pioneer species ‚Üí intermediate ‚Üí climax community
    - **Secondary**: After disturbance (fire, agriculture)
      - Faster than primary (soil present)
    - **Facilitation**: Early species make conditions favorable for later
    - **Inhibition**: Early species prevent establishment of later
    - **Tolerance**: Later species tolerate conditions, outcompete earlier
- **Ecosystem Ecology**: Energy flow and nutrient cycling
  - **Energy Flow**: One-way through ecosystem (not recycled)
    - **Trophic levels**: Producers ‚Üí primary consumers ‚Üí secondary consumers ‚Üí tertiary consumers
    - **10% rule**: ~10% energy transferred to next level
      - Most lost as heat (2nd law of thermodynamics)
    - **Biomass pyramid**: Usually decreases at higher levels
      - Exceptions: inverted in some aquatic (phytoplankton reproduce rapidly)
    - **Food chains**: Linear pathway
    - **Food webs**: Interconnected food chains (more realistic)
    - **Decomposers**: Recycle nutrients from all levels
  - **Primary Productivity**: Rate of biomass production
    - **GPP** (Gross Primary Productivity): Total photosynthesis
    - **NPP** (Net Primary Productivity): GPP - respiration
      - Available to consumers
    - Factors limiting: light, water, nutrients, temperature
    - Highest: tropical rainforests, estuaries, coral reefs
  - **Nutrient Cycling**: Matter recycled (unlike energy)
    - **Carbon cycle**:
      - Photosynthesis removes CO‚ÇÇ, respiration releases CO‚ÇÇ
      - Fossil fuel burning adds CO‚ÇÇ (anthropogenic)
      - Ocean and forest carbon sinks
    - **Nitrogen cycle**:
      - **N‚ÇÇ fixation**: N‚ÇÇ ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ (bacteria: Rhizobium, lightning)
      - **Nitrification**: NH‚ÇÉ ‚Üí NO‚ÇÇ‚Åª ‚Üí NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª (bacteria)
      - **Assimilation**: Plants uptake NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, make proteins
      - **Ammonification**: Decomposers release NH‚ÇÉ from organic matter
      - **Denitrification**: NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª ‚Üí N‚ÇÇ (bacteria)
      - Human impact: Haber process (fertilizers), excess runoff
    - **Phosphorus cycle**:
      - Weathering releases from rocks
      - No atmospheric component
      - Often limiting in aquatic ecosystems
    - **Water cycle**: Evaporation ‚Üí condensation ‚Üí precipitation ‚Üí runoff/infiltration
- **Biomes**: Large-scale terrestrial ecosystems
  - Determined by climate (temperature and precipitation)
  - Tropical rainforest, temperate forest, grassland, desert, tundra, etc.
- **Conservation Biology**: Protecting biodiversity
  - **Biodiversity levels**: Genetic, species, ecosystem
  - **Threats** (HIPPO):
    - **Habitat loss**: #1 threat (deforestation, urbanization)
    - **Invasive species**: Outcompete natives
    - **Pollution**: Air, water, soil contamination
    - **Population growth**: Increased human demand
    - **Overexploitation**: Overfishing, overhunting
  - **Climate change**: Shifts ranges, phenology, ocean acidification
  - **Strategies**:
    - Protected areas (national parks, reserves)
    - Habitat restoration
    - Captive breeding and reintroduction
    - Corridors: connect fragmented habitats
    - Sustainable resource management
    - International agreements (CITES, CBD)
  - **Ecosystem services**: Benefits from nature
    - Provisioning (food, water), regulating (climate, floods), supporting (nutrient cycling), cultural (recreation)

**Ecological Principles**:
- Energy flows one-way, matter cycles
- Populations limited by carrying capacity and limiting factors
- Species interactions shape community structure
- Ecosystems are dynamic, not static
- Biodiversity increases ecosystem stability and resilience

**Conceptual Insights**:
- Ecology integrates all biological levels
- Bottom-up control (resources) vs top-down control (predators)
- Disturbances can increase diversity (intermediate disturbance hypothesis)
- No single species exists in isolation
- Human activities now dominant force in most ecosystems
- Conservation requires understanding ecology

**Key Applications**: Conservation, wildlife management, agriculture, climate change mitigation, sustainability, fisheries management, ecosystem restoration, environmental policy

### 5. Animal & Plant Biology
Structure and function of organisms across all systems:

**Plant Structure & Function**:
- **Roots**: Absorption (root hairs increase surface area), anchorage, storage; types: taproot vs fibrous
- **Stems**: Support, transport (xylem for water/minerals ‚Üë, phloem for sugars ‚Üì), storage; vascular tissue arrangement
- **Leaves**: Photosynthesis site, gas exchange (stomata with guard cells), transpiration; anatomy: epidermis, mesophyll (palisade/spongy), veins
- **Meristems**: Apical (primary growth/length) vs lateral/cambium (secondary growth/width)

**Plant Reproduction**:
- **Flowers**: Complete (4 whorls: sepals, petals, stamens, carpels) vs incomplete; perfect vs imperfect
- **Pollination**: Self vs cross; vectors: wind, insects, birds, bats
- **Double fertilization**: Sperm + egg ‚Üí zygote (2n); sperm + polar nuclei ‚Üí endosperm (3n)
- **Seeds**: Embryo + stored food + seed coat; dispersal mechanisms
- **Alternation of generations**: Sporophyte (2n, dominant in seed plants) ‚Üî Gametophyte (n, reduced)

**Animal Organ Systems** (8 major systems):
1. **Circulatory**: Heart (4 chambers in mammals/birds), blood vessels (arteries‚Üíarterioles‚Üícapillaries‚Üívenules‚Üíveins), blood (RBC, WBC, platelets, plasma); cardiac cycle (systole/diastole), blood pressure
2. **Respiratory**: Gas exchange surface; lungs (alveoli maximize surface area), breathing mechanism (diaphragm, intercostals), hemoglobin O‚ÇÇ binding (cooperative, responds to pH/CO‚ÇÇ)
3. **Digestive**: Alimentary canal (mouth‚Üíesophagus‚Üístomach‚Üísmall intestine‚Üílarge intestine); mechanical/chemical digestion, enzymatic breakdown, absorption (villi/microvilli), liver (bile), pancreas (enzymes/hormones)
4. **Excretory**: Kidneys (nephrons: glomerulus, Bowman's capsule, tubules), filtration‚Üíreabsorption‚Üísecretion‚Üíexcretion; osmoregulation, maintain water/salt/pH balance
5. **Nervous**: Neurons (dendrites‚Üísoma‚Üíaxon‚Üíterminals), action potential (Na‚Å∫ in, K‚Å∫ out), synapse (neurotransmitters), CNS (brain/spinal cord) vs PNS (somatic/autonomic); brain regions: cerebrum, cerebellum, brainstem; sensory systems (vision, hearing, etc.)
6. **Endocrine**: Hormones (chemical messengers in blood); glands: pituitary (master), thyroid, adrenals, pancreas (insulin/glucagon), gonads; feedback mechanisms (negative/positive)
7. **Immune**: Innate (barriers, phagocytes, inflammation, complement) vs adaptive (B cells‚Üíantibodies, T cells‚Üícell-mediated); antigen recognition, clonal selection, memory cells, vaccines
8. **Reproductive**: Male (testes‚Üísperm, testosterone) vs female (ovaries‚Üíeggs, estrogen/progesterone); gametogenesis (meiosis), fertilization, development

**Homeostasis**: Maintaining stable internal environment
- **Thermoregulation**: Ectotherms vs endotherms; behavioral/physiological responses
- **Water balance**: Osmoregulation in different environments
- **Blood glucose**: Insulin (lowers) vs glucagon (raises); diabetes
- **pH regulation**: Buffer systems, respiratory/renal compensation

**Key Applications**: Medicine, agriculture, veterinary science, pharmacology, physiology research

### 6. Diversity of Life
Complete survey of life's diversity from viruses to vertebrates:

**Viruses** (Non-living infectious agents):
- **Structure**: Nucleic acid (DNA or RNA, single/double-stranded) + protein coat (capsid) ¬± envelope
- **Replication**: Lytic cycle (immediate) vs lysogenic cycle (integrated prophage)
- **Classification**: By nucleic acid type, shape, host range
- **Examples**: Bacteriophages, influenza, HIV, SARS-CoV-2
- **Evolution**: Rapid mutation, emerging diseases, zoonotic transmission

**Prokaryotes** (Domains Bacteria and Archaea):
- **Bacteria**: Peptidoglycan cell walls, diverse shapes (cocci, bacilli, spirilla), flagella, pili; Gram-positive vs Gram-negative
- **Archaea**: Extremophiles (thermophiles, halophiles, methanogens); unique membrane lipids, no peptidoglycan
- **Metabolism diversity**: Autotrophs (photoautotrophs, chemoautotrophs) vs heterotrophs
- **Ecological roles**: Nitrogen fixation, decomposition, gut microbiome, pathogens
- **Reproduction**: Binary fission; genetic exchange via conjugation, transformation, transduction

**Protists** (Kingdom Protista - paraphyletic):
- **Algae**: Photosynthetic (green, red, brown algae), primary producers
- **Protozoans**: Heterotrophic; groups: amoeboids, flagellates, ciliates, apicomplexans (Plasmodium)
- **Slime molds**: Cellular vs plasmodial; decomposers
- **Ecological importance**: Base of aquatic food webs, symbioses (zooxanthellae in corals)

**Fungi** (Kingdom Fungi):
- **Structure**: Hyphae (filaments) ‚Üí mycelium (network); chitin cell walls; heterotrophic
- **Reproduction**: Asexual (spores, budding) vs sexual (fusion of hyphae, spore formation)
- **Groups**: Chytrids, zygomycetes, ascomycetes (sac fungi), basidiomycetes (club fungi)
- **Ecological roles**: Decomposers (nutrient recycling), mycorrhizae (plant root symbiosis), lichens (fungus-algae symbiosis)
- **Applications**: Food (mushrooms, cheese, bread), antibiotics (penicillin), fermentation

**Plant Diversity** (Kingdom Plantae):
- **Bryophytes** (mosses, liverworts, hornworts): No vascular tissue, gametophyte dominant, require water for reproduction
- **Seedless vascular** (ferns, horsetails, club mosses): Vascular tissue (xylem/phloem), sporophyte dominant, still need water for fertilization
- **Gymnosperms** (conifers, cycads, ginkgo): "Naked seeds" in cones, vascular, pollen (no water needed), wood
- **Angiosperms** (flowering plants): Seeds in fruits, flowers, double fertilization; monocots vs dicots (eudicots)
- **Evolutionary trends**: Gametophyte reduction, independence from water, seeds, flowers/fruits

**Animal Diversity** (Kingdom Animalia):

*Invertebrates* (95% of animal species):
- **Sponges** (Porifera): No true tissues, filter feeders, asymmetrical
- **Cnidarians** (jellyfish, corals, sea anemones): Radial symmetry, two tissue layers, cnidocytes (stinging cells), polyp/medusa forms
- **Flatworms** (Platyhelminthes): Bilateral symmetry begins, acoelomate, some parasitic (tapeworms, flukes)
- **Roundworms** (Nematoda): Pseudocoelomate, complete digestive tract, many parasitic (Ascaris, hookworm)
- **Mollusks** (Mollusca): Soft body, mantle, often shell; classes: gastropods (snails), bivalves (clams), cephalopods (squid, octopus - most intelligent invertebrates)
- **Annelids** (segmented worms): Segmentation, closed circulatory system; earthworms, leeches, polychaetes
- **Arthropods** (Arthropoda - largest phylum): Segmented body, jointed appendages, exoskeleton (chitin), open circulatory; groups:
  - Chelicerates: spiders, scorpions, ticks, horseshoe crabs
  - Crustaceans: crabs, lobsters, shrimp, barnacles
  - Insects: 3 body parts (head/thorax/abdomen), 3 pairs legs, wings; complete/incomplete metamorphosis
  - Myriapods: centipedes, millipedes
- **Echinoderms** (Echinodermata): Radial symmetry (adults), water vascular system, endoskeleton; sea stars, sea urchins, sea cucumbers

*Vertebrates* (Phylum Chordata, Subphylum Vertebrata):
- **Fishes**: 
  - Jawless (lampreys, hagfish): Most primitive
  - Cartilaginous (sharks, rays): Cartilage skeleton, no swim bladder
  - Bony (ray-finned and lobe-finned): Bone skeleton, swim bladder/lungs, operculum
- **Amphibians**: Dual life (aquatic larvae/terrestrial adults), moist skin (gas exchange), external fertilization; frogs, salamanders, caecilians
- **Reptiles**: Amniotic egg, scales (keratin), ectothermic; turtles, lizards, snakes, crocodilians; birds evolved from dinosaurs
- **Birds**: Feathers, wings (modified forelimbs), endothermic, high metabolism, air sacs, hollow bones; flight adaptations
- **Mammals**: Hair, mammary glands, endothermic, specialized teeth, enlarged brain; three groups:
  - Monotremes: Egg-laying (platypus, echidna)
  - Marsupials: Pouched development (kangaroos, opossums)
  - Eutherians (Placentals): Placenta, longer development (most mammals including humans)

**Phylogeny & Systematics**:
- **Taxonomy**: Domain‚ÜíKingdom‚ÜíPhylum‚ÜíClass‚ÜíOrder‚ÜíFamily‚ÜíGenus‚ÜíSpecies
- **Three Domains**: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya (protists, fungi, plants, animals)
- **Phylogenetic trees**: Show evolutionary relationships; cladograms based on shared derived characters (synapomorphies)
- **Molecular systematics**: DNA/protein sequences, molecular clocks estimate divergence times
- **Major evolutionary transitions**: Eukaryotic cells (endosymbiosis), multicellularity, bilateral symmetry, segmentation, notochord/backbone, jaws, limbs, amniotic egg, endothermy

**Key Applications**: Biodiversity conservation, taxonomy, evolutionary biology, pest control, biotechnology, medicine (model organisms)

## üî¨ Molecular Biology Concepts

### DNA Structure & Replication
- **Double helix**: Two antiparallel strands with A-T (2 H-bonds) and G-C (3 H-bonds) base pairing
- **Chargaff's rules**: %A = %T and %G = %C
- **Semiconservative replication**: Each strand serves as template
- **Key enzymes**: Helicase (unwinds), DNA polymerase (synthesizes 5'‚Üí3'), ligase (joins fragments)
- **Leading vs lagging strands**: Continuous vs discontinuous (Okazaki fragments)

### Gene Expression
- **Transcription**: DNA ‚Üí mRNA by RNA polymerase (5'‚Üí3' synthesis)
  - Promoter: RNA polymerase binding site (TATA box)
  - Template strand read 3'‚Üí5'
- **RNA processing (eukaryotes)**: 5' cap, 3' poly-A tail, splicing (introns removed)
- **Translation**: mRNA ‚Üí protein at ribosomes
  - Genetic code: 64 codons (61 amino acids + 3 stop: UAA, UAG, UGA)
  - Start codon: AUG (methionine)
  - tRNA anticodons pair with mRNA codons

### Gene Regulation
- **Prokaryotes**: Operons (lac and trp)
  - Negative control: Repressor blocks transcription
  - Positive control: Activator promotes transcription
- **Eukaryotes**: 
  - Chromatin remodeling (histone acetylation)
  - Transcription factors
  - Enhancers and silencers
  - Epigenetics: DNA methylation, histone modification

### Mutations
- **Point mutations**: Silent, missense, nonsense
- **Frameshift**: Insertion/deletion (not multiple of 3)
- **Chromosomal**: Deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation

## üß™ Cell Biology

### Cell Membrane & Transport
- **Fluid mosaic model**: Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins
- **Passive transport**: No ATP required
  - Simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, osmosis
- **Active transport**: Requires ATP (Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ pump)
- **Bulk transport**: Endocytosis, exocytosis
- **Tonicity**: Isotonic, hypertonic (crenation), hypotonic (lysis/turgor)

### Cellular Respiration
**Overall equation**: $C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{ATP}$

- **Glycolysis** (cytoplasm): Glucose ‚Üí 2 pyruvate; Net: 2 ATP, 2 NADH
- **Pyruvate oxidation**: 2 pyruvate ‚Üí 2 acetyl-CoA + 2 CO‚ÇÇ; 2 NADH
- **Krebs cycle** (mitochondrial matrix): Per glucose: 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH‚ÇÇ, 4 CO‚ÇÇ
- **Electron transport chain**: ~32-34 ATP via oxidative phosphorylation
- **Total**: ~36-38 ATP per glucose

### Photosynthesis
**Overall equation**: $6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{light} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$

- **Light reactions** (thylakoid): Light ‚Üí ATP + NADPH
  - Water splitting: $2H_2O \rightarrow 4H^+ + O_2 + 4e^-$
  - Non-cyclic: ATP + NADPH; Cyclic: ATP only
- **Calvin cycle** (stroma): CO‚ÇÇ fixation
  - Rubisco fixes CO‚ÇÇ to RuBP ‚Üí 3-PGA
  - 3 CO‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 1 G3P (3-carbon sugar)
  - 6 turns to make 1 glucose

### Fermentation
- **Alcohol**: Pyruvate ‚Üí ethanol + CO‚ÇÇ
- **Lactic acid**: Pyruvate ‚Üí lactate
- **Net**: 2 ATP per glucose (glycolysis only, regenerates NAD‚Å∫)

### Cell Division
- **Mitosis**: 2 identical diploid cells (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase, Cytokinesis)
- **Meiosis**: 4 non-identical haploid gametes
  - Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes separate (crossing over)
  - Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate
- **Variation sources**: Crossing over, independent assortment, random fertilization

## üß† Genetics

### Mendelian Genetics
- **Law of Segregation**: Alleles separate during gamete formation
- **Law of Independent Assortment**: Different genes assort independently
- **Dominant vs recessive**: Expressed in heterozygotes vs homozygotes only
- **Punnett squares**: Predict offspring genotypes and phenotypes
  - Monohybrid: 3:1 ratio; Dihybrid: 9:3:3:1 ratio

### Beyond Mendel
- **Incomplete dominance**: Heterozygote intermediate phenotype
- **Codominance**: Both alleles expressed (ABO blood type)
- **Multiple alleles**: >2 alleles in population
- **Pleiotropy**: One gene affects multiple traits
- **Epistasis**: One gene masks another
- **Polygenic inheritance**: Multiple genes affect one trait (height, skin color)

### Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
**Allele frequencies**: $p + q = 1$
**Genotype frequencies**: $p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$
- $p^2$ = frequency of AA
- $2pq$ = frequency of Aa
- $q^2$ = frequency of aa
- **Assumptions**: No mutation, random mating, no selection, no gene flow, large population
- **Use**: Detect if evolution is occurring

## üå± Evolution

### Natural Selection
- **Variation**: Individuals differ in traits
- **Heritability**: Traits passed to offspring
- **Differential reproduction**: Some survive/reproduce better
- **Result**: Allele frequencies change over time

### Types of Selection
- **Directional**: Favors one extreme
- **Stabilizing**: Favors intermediate
- **Disruptive**: Favors both extremes

### Evidence for Evolution
1. Fossil record (transitional forms)
2. Comparative anatomy (homologous/analogous structures)
3. Comparative embryology
4. Molecular biology (DNA/protein similarities)
5. Biogeography (species distribution)

### Speciation
- **Allopatric**: Geographic isolation
- **Sympatric**: No geographic barrier (polyploidy)
- **Reproductive isolation**: Prezygotic and postzygotic barriers

### Population Genetics
- **Genetic drift**: Random changes (bottleneck, founder effects)
- **Gene flow**: Allele movement between populations
- **Mutation**: Ultimate source of variation

## üåç Ecology

### Energy Flow
- **Trophic levels**: Producers ‚Üí primary consumers ‚Üí secondary consumers ‚Üí decomposers
- **10% rule**: ~10% energy transferred between levels
- **Biomass pyramid**: Decreases at higher levels

### Nutrient Cycling
- **Carbon cycle**: Photosynthesis ‚Üî respiration; fossil fuels
- **Nitrogen cycle**: N‚ÇÇ fixation ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ/NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ ‚Üí NO‚ÇÇ‚Åª ‚Üí NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª ‚Üí denitrification
- **Phosphorus cycle**: Weathering, no atmospheric component
- **Water cycle**: Evaporation ‚Üí condensation ‚Üí precipitation

### Population Ecology
**Exponential growth**: $\frac{dN}{dt} = r_{max}N$ (J-shaped)
**Logistic growth**: $\frac{dN}{dt} = r_{max}N\left(\frac{K-N}{K}\right)$ (S-shaped)
- K = carrying capacity
- **Density-dependent**: Competition, predation, disease
- **Density-independent**: Weather, disasters
- **Life history**: r-selected (many offspring) vs K-selected (few offspring, parental care)

### Community Ecology
- **Competition**: Both species harmed; competitive exclusion
- **Predation**: Consumer-resource; coevolution
- **Symbiosis**: Mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), parasitism (+/-)
- **Keystone species**: Disproportionate impact
- **Succession**: Primary (bare rock) vs secondary (disturbance)

### Conservation
- **Threats**: Habitat loss, invasive species, pollution, climate change, overexploitation
- **Strategies**: Protected areas, restoration, captive breeding
