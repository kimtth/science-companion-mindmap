# Chemistry Terminology

## Core Concepts from Chemistry Mindmaps

### Atomic Structure
- **Atom**: Smallest unit of an element retaining its chemical properties
- **Subatomic Particle**: Particles smaller than atoms (protons, neutrons, electrons)
- **Electron**: Negatively charged particle orbiting the atomic nucleus
- **Proton**: Positively charged particle in the atomic nucleus
- **Neutron**: Neutral particle in the atomic nucleus
- **Isotope**: Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons
- **Electron Configuration**: Arrangement of electrons in atomic orbitals
- **Orbital**: Region of space where an electron is likely to be found
- **Periodic Table**: Tabular arrangement of elements by atomic number and properties
- **Periodic Trends**: Patterns in element properties across the periodic table
- **Atomic Number**: Number of protons in an atom's nucleus
- **Mass Number**: Total number of protons and neutrons in a nucleus
- **Valence Electron**: Electron in the outermost shell involved in bonding

#### Subatomic Particles
- **Proton Charge**: +1 elementary charge (1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
- **Proton Mass**: 1.6726 × 10⁻²⁷ kg (~1.0073 amu)
- **Neutron Mass**: Slightly heavier than proton (~1.0087 amu)
- **Neutron Stability**: Stable in nucleus; free neutron decays in ~10 minutes
- **Electron Charge**: -1 elementary charge; equal and opposite to proton
- **Electron Mass**: 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg (1/1836 of proton mass)
- **Electron Cloud**: Probability distribution determining atomic size
- **Atomic Nucleus**: Central core containing protons and neutrons
- **Strong Nuclear Force**: Binds nucleons; overcomes electrostatic repulsion
- **Nuclear Density**: Extremely dense (~10¹⁷ kg/m³)

#### Isotopes and Radioactivity
- **Isotope Notation**: Symbol with mass and atomic number (A-Z-X or X-A)
- **Stable Isotopes**: Do not decay; specific neutron-to-proton ratios
- **Radioactivity**: Unstable isotopes decay; emit radiation
- **Alpha Decay**: Emits helium nucleus; mass decreases by 4
- **Beta Decay**: Neutron to proton or vice versa; emits electron or positron
- **Gamma Decay**: High-energy photon emission; no change in mass or atomic number
- **Half-Life**: Time for half of sample to decay; characteristic for each isotope
- **Atomic Mass**: Weighted average of isotopes based on natural abundance
- **Mass Spectrometry**: Measures isotope abundance; determines precise atomic masses

#### Quantum Numbers and Orbitals
- **Principal Quantum Number (n)**: Energy level, shell; n = 1, 2, 3, ...
- **Angular Momentum Quantum Number (l)**: Subshell type; l = 0 to n-1 (s,p,d,f)
- **Magnetic Quantum Number (mₗ)**: Orbital orientation; mₗ = -l to +l
- **Spin Quantum Number (mₛ)**: Electron spin; +½ or -½
- **s Orbital**: Spherical shape; holds 2 electrons
- **p Orbital**: Dumbbell shape; three orientations, 6 electrons total
- **d Orbital**: Complex shapes; five orientations, 10 electrons total
- **f Orbital**: Very complex shapes; seven orientations, 14 electrons total
- **Orbital Energy Levels**: 1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d (filling order)
- **Aufbau Principle**: Fill lowest energy orbitals first
- **Pauli Exclusion Principle**: Maximum two electrons per orbital with opposite spins
- **Hund's Rule**: Maximize unpaired electrons; fill degenerate orbitals singly first
- **Core Electrons**: Inner shell electrons; generally not involved in bonding
- **Noble Gas Configuration**: Core electrons abbreviated; e.g., [Ar] 4s² 3d¹⁰
- **Octet Rule**: Atoms tend toward 8 valence electrons; stable noble gas configuration

### Chemical Bonding
- **Chemical Bond**: Force holding atoms together in molecules or compounds
- **Ionic Bond**: Bond formed by transfer of electrons between atoms
- **Covalent Bond**: Bond formed by sharing electrons between atoms
- **Metallic Bond**: Bond between metal atoms involving delocalized electrons
- **Lewis Structure**: Diagram showing valence electrons and bonding in molecules
- **VSEPR**: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion theory for predicting molecular geometry
- **Molecular Geometry**: Three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in a molecule
- **Polarity**: Unequal distribution of electron density in a molecule
- **Electronegativity**: Measure of an atom's ability to attract electrons
- **Intermolecular Forces**: Forces between molecules (van der Waals, H-bonds)
- **Hydrogen Bond**: Strong dipole-dipole interaction involving hydrogen
- **Dipole**: Separation of positive and negative charges in a molecule

### Stoichiometry & States
- **Stoichiometry**: Quantitative relationships between reactants and products
- **Mole**: Amount of substance containing Avogadro's number of particles
- **Molar Mass**: Mass of one mole of a substance
- **Avogadro's Number**: 6.022 × 10²³ particles per mole
- **Limiting Reactant**: Reactant that is completely consumed, limiting product formation
- **Balancing Equations**: Ensuring equal numbers of each atom on both sides
- **States of Matter**: Physical forms (solid, liquid, gas, plasma)
- **Gas Laws**: Relationships between pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas
- **Ideal Gas Law**: PV = nRT equation describing gas behavior
- **Phase Change**: Transition between states of matter
- **Vapor Pressure**: Pressure exerted by vapor in equilibrium with its liquid

### Thermochemistry & Kinetics
- **Thermochemistry**: Study of energy changes in chemical reactions
- **Enthalpy**: Heat content of a system at constant pressure
- **Entropy**: Measure of disorder or randomness in a system
- **Gibbs Free Energy**: Energy available to do work in a system
- **Hess's Law**: Total enthalpy change is independent of pathway
- **Calorimetry**: Measurement of heat changes in chemical reactions
- **Exothermic**: Reaction releasing energy as heat
- **Endothermic**: Reaction absorbing energy as heat
- **Reaction Rate**: Speed at which reactants are converted to products
- **Activation Energy**: Minimum energy required for a reaction to occur
- **Catalyst**: Substance that increases reaction rate without being consumed
- **Reaction Mechanism**: Step-by-step sequence of elementary reactions
- **Rate Law**: Mathematical relationship between reaction rate and concentrations

### Equilibrium
- **Chemical Equilibrium**: State where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal
- **Equilibrium Constant (K)**: Ratio of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium
- **Le Châtelier's Principle**: System at equilibrium shifts to counteract disturbances
- **ICE Table**: Initial, Change, Equilibrium method for solving equilibrium problems
- **Reaction Quotient (Q)**: Ratio at non-equilibrium conditions
- **Solubility**: Maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a solvent
- **Solubility Product (Ksp)**: Equilibrium constant for dissolution of ionic compounds
- **Common Ion Effect**: Decreased solubility due to presence of common ion
- **Dynamic Equilibrium**: State where forward and reverse processes occur at equal rates

### Acids & Bases
- **Acid**: Substance that donates protons (H⁺) or accepts electron pairs
- **Base**: Substance that accepts protons or donates electron pairs
- **pH**: Measure of acidity/basicity on a logarithmic scale
- **Neutralization**: Reaction between acid and base forming salt and water
- **Strong Acid/Base**: Completely ionizes in solution
- **Weak Acid/Base**: Partially ionizes in solution
- **Buffer**: Solution resisting pH changes when acid or base is added
- **Titration**: Analytical technique to determine concentration by neutralization
- **Equivalence Point**: Point where acid and base are stoichiometrically equal
- **Indicator**: Substance that changes color at specific pH
- **Conjugate Acid-Base Pair**: Acid and base differing by one proton
- **Arrhenius Theory**: Acids produce H⁺, bases produce OH⁻
- **Brønsted-Lowry Theory**: Acids donate protons, bases accept protons
- **Lewis Theory**: Acids accept electron pairs, bases donate electron pairs

### Redox & Electrochemistry
- **Oxidation**: Loss of electrons, increase in oxidation state
- **Reduction**: Gain of electrons, decrease in oxidation state
- **Redox Reaction**: Reaction involving electron transfer
- **Oxidation State**: Charge an atom would have if all bonds were ionic
- **Oxidizing Agent**: Species that accepts electrons (gets reduced)
- **Reducing Agent**: Species that donates electrons (gets oxidized)
- **Half-Reaction**: Either oxidation or reduction part of redox reaction
- **Galvanic Cell**: Electrochemical cell producing electricity from spontaneous reaction
- **Electrolytic Cell**: Cell using electricity to drive non-spontaneous reaction
- **Electrode**: Conductor through which current enters or leaves a cell
- **Anode**: Electrode where oxidation occurs
- **Cathode**: Electrode where reduction occurs
- **Cell Potential**: Voltage difference between electrodes
- **Nernst Equation**: Relates cell potential to concentrations
- **Electroplating**: Depositing metal coating using electrolysis

### Organic Chemistry
- **Hydrocarbon**: Compound containing only carbon and hydrogen
- **Alkane**: Saturated hydrocarbon with single bonds (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂)
- **Alkene**: Hydrocarbon with carbon-carbon double bonds (CₙH₂ₙ)
- **Alkyne**: Hydrocarbon with carbon-carbon triple bonds (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂)
- **Aromatic**: Compounds containing benzene ring or similar structure
- **Functional Group**: Specific group of atoms determining chemical properties
- **Alcohol**: Organic compound with hydroxyl group (-OH)
- **Aldehyde**: Compound with carbonyl group at end of carbon chain (-CHO)
- **Ketone**: Compound with carbonyl group in middle of chain (C=O)
- **Carboxylic Acid**: Compound with carboxyl group (-COOH)
- **Ester**: Compound formed from acid and alcohol (-COO-)
- **Amine**: Organic derivative of ammonia (-NH₂)
- **Isomer**: Compounds with same molecular formula but different structures
- **Polymer**: Large molecule made of repeating units (monomers)
- **Biological Molecule**: Organic compounds essential for life (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids)

### Transition Metals & Nuclear
- **Transition Metal**: Elements in d-block of periodic table
- **Coordination Compound**: Complex with central metal atom bonded to ligands
- **Ligand**: Ion or molecule bonded to central metal atom
- **Complex Ion**: Ion containing metal center and attached ligands
- **Colored Compound**: Substance that absorbs visible light (common in transition metals)
- **Catalysis**: Process of increasing reaction rate using a catalyst
- **Radioactivity**: Spontaneous emission of radiation from unstable nuclei
- **Nuclear Fission**: Splitting of heavy nucleus into lighter nuclei
- **Nuclear Fusion**: Combining light nuclei into heavier nucleus
- **Half-Life**: Time required for half of radioactive sample to decay
- **Alpha Particle**: Helium nucleus emitted in radioactive decay
- **Beta Particle**: Electron or positron emitted in radioactive decay
- **Gamma Ray**: High-energy photon emitted in radioactive decay
- **Isotope**: Atoms with same atomic number but different mass numbers

## Key Properties & Measurements

#### Periodic Table - Groups and Blocks
- **Group 1 - Alkali Metals**: Highly reactive metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr)
- **Group 2 - Alkaline Earth**: Reactive metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra)
- **Groups 3-12 - Transition Metals**: Variable oxidation states; d-block elements
- **Group 17 - Halogens**: Highly reactive nonmetals (F, Cl, Br, I, At)
- **Group 18 - Noble Gases**: Inert, stable octet (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn)
- **s-Block**: Groups 1-2; valence electrons in s orbital
- **p-Block**: Groups 13-18; valence electrons in p orbital
- **d-Block**: Transition metals; filling d orbitals
- **f-Block**: Lanthanides and actinides; filling f orbitals
- **Period**: Horizontal row; period number = highest shell

#### Periodic Trends
- **Atomic Radius**: Size of atom; decreases across period, increases down group
- **Ionic Radius**: Size of ion; cations smaller, anions larger than parent atom
- **Covalent Radius**: Half of bond length between identical atoms
- **Van der Waals Radius**: Half of distance between non-bonded atoms; largest radius measure
- **Ionization Energy**: Energy to remove electron; increases across, decreases down
- **First Ionization Energy**: Remove first electron; always endothermic
- **Successive Ionization**: Each subsequent electron harder; large jumps removing core electrons
- **Electronegativity**: Attraction for electrons; increases across, decreases down
- **Pauling Scale**: Most common electronegativity scale; F = 4.0 (highest)
- **Electronegativity Difference**: Predicts bond type; large difference → ionic
- **Electron Affinity**: Energy change adding electron; generally more negative across period
- **Exothermic EA**: Energy released adding electron; most nonmetals
- **Endothermic EA**: Energy required adding electron; noble gases, some metals
- **Metallic Character**: Tendency to lose electrons; decreases across, increases down

### Physical Properties
- **Density**: Mass per unit volume
- **Melting Point**: Temperature at which solid becomes liquid
- **Boiling Point**: Temperature at which liquid becomes gas
- **Solubility**: Ability of substance to dissolve in solvent
- **Conductivity**: Ability to conduct electricity or heat

### Chemical Properties
- **Reactivity**: Tendency of substance to undergo chemical reactions
- **Stability**: Resistance to chemical change
- **Acidity**: Ability to donate protons
- **Basicity**: Ability to accept protons

### Thermodynamic Properties
- **Heat Capacity**: Amount of heat needed to raise temperature
- **Specific Heat**: Heat capacity per unit mass
- **Latent Heat**: Energy for phase change at constant temperature

## Chemical Bonding

### Ionic Bonding Details
- **Lattice Energy**: Energy released when gaseous ions form solid ionic compound; large for small, highly charged ions
- **Born-Haber Cycle**: Thermochemical cycle relating lattice energy to other properties
- **Ionic Crystal Structure**: Regular 3D arrangement (NaCl cubic, CsCl body-centered cubic)
- **Coordination Number in Ionic Solids**: Number of nearest neighbors of opposite charge
- **Ionic Compound Properties**: High melting/boiling points, brittle, conduct electricity when molten or dissolved

### Covalent Bonding Details
- **Sigma (σ) Bond**: Head-on orbital overlap; single bonds are all σ; free rotation
- **Pi (π) Bond**: Side-by-side p-orbital overlap; in double/triple bonds; restricts rotation
- **Bond Length**: Distance between nuclei; triple < double < single for same atoms
- **Bond Energy**: Energy to break bond; triple > double > single
- **Bond Order**: (Bonding electrons - Antibonding electrons)/2; higher = stronger, shorter
- **Polar Covalent**: Unequal sharing; 0.4 < ΔEN < 1.7
- **Nonpolar Covalent**: Equal sharing; ΔEN < 0.4
- **Ionic Character**: ΔEN > 1.7; high electronegativity difference

### Lewis Structures Advanced
- **Octet Expansion**: Period 3+ elements can exceed octet using d orbitals (SF₆, PCl₅)
- **Incomplete Octet**: Some stable with < 8 electrons (BF₃ has 6, radicals have unpaired)
- **Resonance Structures**: Multiple valid Lewis structures; double-headed arrow between them
- **Resonance Hybrid**: Actual structure is blend; all bonds intermediate between single and double
- **Formal Charge Calculation**: FC = Valence e⁻ - Nonbonding e⁻ - ½(Bonding e⁻)
- **Formal Charge Guidelines**: Minimize formal charges; negative on more electronegative atoms
- **Coordinate Covalent Bond**: Both electrons from one atom (dative bond)

### Molecular Geometry Complete
- **Steric Number**: Number of electron groups (bonding + lone pairs) around central atom
- **AXₙEₘ Notation**: A=central atom, X=bonded atoms, E=lone pairs
- **Bent Molecular Shape**: Can arise from tetrahedral (AX₂E₂) or trigonal planar (AX₂E)
- **Lone Pair Repulsion**: LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP; lone pairs take more space
- **Bond Angle Deviation**: Lone pairs compress bond angles (H₂O: 104.5° < 109.5°)

### Hybridization Complete
- **sp Hybrid Orbitals**: Two equivalent orbitals; 50% s, 50% p; linear 180°
- **sp² Hybrid Orbitals**: Three equivalent orbitals; 33% s, 67% p; trigonal planar 120°
- **sp³ Hybrid Orbitals**: Four equivalent orbitals; 25% s, 75% p; tetrahedral 109.5°
- **sp³d Hybrid Orbitals**: Five orbitals using one d orbital; trigonal bipyramidal
- **sp³d² Hybrid Orbitals**: Six orbitals using two d orbitals; octahedral
- **Sigma Framework**: All single bonds use hybrid orbitals
- **Pi Bonds from Unhybridized p**: Double bond = 1σ + 1π; Triple = 1σ + 2π

### Intermolecular Forces Complete
- **London Dispersion Forces**: Present in all molecules; only force in nonpolar; increase with size/electrons
- **Instantaneous Dipole**: Temporary from electron movement
- **Induced Dipole**: Dipole created in neighboring molecule
- **Dipole-Dipole Strength**: Stronger than London; ~5% of ionic bond strength
- **Hydrogen Bond Strength**: Strongest IMF (~10% ionic bond); O-H⋯O, N-H⋯N, F-H⋯F
- **Hydrogen Bond Requirements**: H bonded to F, O, or N; interacts with lone pair on F, O, or N
- **Ion-Dipole**: Strongest IMF involving neutral molecules; important in solution chemistry
- **Boiling Point Trends**: Higher IMF → higher BP; H-bonding > dipole-dipole > dispersion

## Stoichiometry & Gas Laws

### Mole Calculations
- **Mole Conversions**: Use molar mass, Avogadro's number, or molar volume (22.4 L at STP)
- **Empirical Formula**: Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms
- **Molecular Formula**: Actual number of atoms; multiple of empirical formula
- **Combustion Analysis**: Determine formula from masses of CO₂ and H₂O produced
- **Percent Composition from Formula**: (Mass of element / Molar mass) × 100%

### Stoichiometric Problems
- **Mole Ratio**: From coefficients in balanced equation
- **Mass-to-Mass Calculations**: Convert to moles, use mole ratio, convert back to mass
- **Limiting Reactant Identification**: Calculate product from each reactant; smallest is from LR
- **Excess Reactant Calculation**: Subtract amount consumed from initial amount
- **Consecutive Reactions**: Product of one becomes reactant of next
- **Solution Stoichiometry**: Use molarity (M = mol/L) in calculations

### Gas Law Applications
- **Standard Molar Volume**: 22.4 L/mol at STP (0°C, 1 atm)
- **Gas Density**: d = PM/RT from ideal gas law
- **Molar Mass from Gas Density**: M = dRT/P
- **Partial Pressure**: Pressure exerted by one gas in mixture
- **Mole Fraction (χ)**: χᵢ = nᵢ/n_total; dimensionless
- **Dalton's Law**: P_total = χ₁P_total + χ₂P_total + ...; also P_total = ΣPᵢ
- **Vapor Pressure**: Pressure of vapor above liquid at equilibrium
- **Collecting Gas Over Water**: P_total = P_gas + P_H₂O; correct for water vapor pressure
- **Graham's Law**: Effusion/diffusion rate inversely proportional to √M
- **Real Gas Deviations**: Occur at high pressure (volume matters) and low temperature (attractions matter)
- **van der Waals Equation**: (P + an²/V²)(V - nb) = nRT; corrects for real gas behavior

### Kinetic Molecular Theory Details
- **KMT Postulates**: Gas particles in constant random motion, negligible volume, elastic collisions
- **Average KE**: KE_avg = (3/2)RT per mole or (3/2)kT per molecule
- **Temperature Definition**: Measure of average kinetic energy of particles
- **Pressure Origin**: Collisions of particles with container walls
- **Root Mean Square Speed**: v_rms = √(3RT/M); most probable, average, and rms speeds differ
- **Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution**: Range of speeds at given temperature; broadens at higher T

### Phase Diagrams
- **Phase Boundary Lines**: Solid-liquid (melting), liquid-gas (vaporization), solid-gas (sublimation)
- **Triple Point**: Unique T and P where three phases coexist
- **Critical Point**: End of liquid-gas boundary; T_c and P_c
- **Supercritical Fluid**: Above critical point; properties of both liquid and gas
- **Normal Melting/Boiling Point**: At 1 atm pressure
- **Clausius-Clapeyron Equation**: ln(P₂/P₁) = -(ΔH_vap/R)(1/T₂ - 1/T₁); vapor pressure vs temperature
- **Water Anomaly**: Negative slope for solid-liquid line; ice less dense than water

### Solution Chemistry
- **Solvation Process**: Solute particles surrounded by solvent; breaks solute-solute and solvent-solvent interactions
- **Enthalpy of Solution**: ΔH_soln = ΔH_solute + ΔH_solvent + ΔH_mix
- **Exothermic Dissolution**: Solution gets warmer; ΔH_soln < 0
- **Endothermic Dissolution**: Solution gets cooler; ΔH_soln > 0
- **Entropy of Solution**: Usually positive; disorder increases
- **Temperature Effect on Solubility**: Most solids more soluble at higher T; gases less soluble
- **Pressure Effect on Solubility**: Henry's Law: C = kP; gases more soluble at higher pressure
- **Electrolyte**: Substance that conducts electricity in solution; dissociates into ions
- **Strong Electrolyte**: Completely dissociates (strong acids, bases, soluble salts)
- **Weak Electrolyte**: Partially dissociates (weak acids, bases)
- **Nonelectrolyte**: Doesn't dissociate; no conductivity (molecular compounds)

### Concentration Units
- **Molarity (M)**: Moles of solute per liter of solution (mol/L)
- **Molality (m)**: Moles of solute per kilogram of solvent (mol/kg)
- **Mole Fraction (χ)**: Moles of component / total moles
- **Mass Percent**: (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) × 100%
- **Volume Percent**: (Volume of solute / Volume of solution) × 100%
- **Parts Per Million (ppm)**: Milligrams of solute per liter of solution (mg/L)
- **Parts Per Billion (ppb)**: Micrograms of solute per liter (μg/L)
- **Dilution**: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂; adding solvent decreases concentration

### Colligative Properties Details
- **Freezing Point Depression**: ΔT_f = K_f × m × i; m = molality, i = van't Hoff factor
- **Boiling Point Elevation**: ΔT_b = K_b × m × i; solution boils at higher temperature
- **K_f and K_b**: Constants depending on solvent (water: K_f = 1.86 °C/m, K_b = 0.512 °C/m)
- **van't Hoff Factor (i)**: Accounts for dissociation; NaCl i≈2, CaCl₂ i≈3, glucose i=1
- **Osmotic Pressure**: π = iMRT; pressure to prevent osmosis
- **Hypertonic Solution**: Higher solute concentration; water flows in
- **Hypotonic Solution**: Lower solute concentration; water flows out
- **Isotonic Solution**: Equal concentration; no net water flow

## Thermochemistry & Kinetics

### Energy and Enthalpy
- **System vs Surroundings**: System = studied part; Surroundings = everything else
- **Open/Closed/Isolated Systems**: Open exchanges matter & energy; Closed exchanges energy only; Isolated exchanges neither
- **State Functions**: H, S, G, U, T, P, V - depend only on current state, not path
- **Path Functions**: q, w - depend on route taken
- **First Law**: ΔE = q + w; energy conserved
- **Enthalpy Definition**: H = E + PV; heat content at constant pressure
- **Standard Conditions**: 1 atm, 25°C (298 K), 1 M for aqueous
- **ΔH°_f (Formation)**: From elements in standard states; elements = 0
- **ΔH°_combustion**: Complete burning in O₂
- **Hess's Law**: ΔH°_rxn = ΣΔH°_f(products) - ΣΔH°_f(reactants)
- **Bond Enthalpy Method**: ΔH = Σ(bonds broken) - Σ(bonds formed)
- **Calorimetry**: Coffee cup (constant P, measures ΔH); Bomb (constant V, measures ΔE)
- **q = mcΔT**: For temperature changes; c = specific heat

### Entropy and Free Energy
- **Entropy (S)**: Measure of disorder; J/(mol·K)
- **Second Law**: ΔS_universe > 0 for spontaneous processes
- **Third Law**: S = 0 for perfect crystal at 0 K
- **ΔS°_rxn = ΣS°(products) - ΣS°(reactants)**
- **Entropy Increases**: Solid → liquid → gas; fewer → more particles; lower → higher T; mixing
- **Gibbs Free Energy**: G = H - TS; energy available for work
- **Spontaneity**: ΔG < 0 spontaneous; ΔG = 0 equilibrium; ΔG > 0 nonspontaneous
- **ΔG° = ΔH° - TΔS°**: Temperature-dependent spontaneity
- **Four Cases**: 
  - ΔH<0, ΔS>0: Always spontaneous
  - ΔH>0, ΔS<0: Never spontaneous
  - ΔH<0, ΔS<0: Low T spontaneous
  - ΔH>0, ΔS>0: High T spontaneous
- **ΔG° and K**: ΔG° = -RT ln K
- **ΔG° and E°**: ΔG° = -nFE°_cell

### Reaction Rates
- **Rate = -(1/a)(Δ[A]/Δt)**: Stoichiometric factors
- **Initial Rate Method**: Determine rate law by comparing rates with different [concentrations]
- **Rate Law**: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n; m, n determined experimentally
- **Zero Order**: [A] = [A]₀ - kt; t₁/₂ = [A]₀/(2k)
- **First Order**: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ - kt; t₁/₂ = 0.693/k (constant half-life)
- **Second Order**: 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt; t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀)
- **Pseudo-First-Order**: One reactant in large excess

### Collision Theory
- **Effective Collision Requirements**: Proper orientation + E ≥ E_a
- **Activation Energy**: Barrier height on energy diagram
- **Transition State**: Highest energy point; activated complex
- **Energy Profile**: Shows E vs reaction progress
- **Catalyst Lowers E_a**: Doesn't change ΔH; increases both forward and reverse rates
- **Arrhenius Equation**: k = Ae^(-E_a/RT)
- **Linearized Form**: ln k = ln A - E_a/RT; plot ln k vs 1/T
- **Temperature Effect**: Rate approximately doubles per 10°C

### Catalysis
- **Catalyst Provides Alternative Path**: Lower E_a mechanism
- **Homogeneous**: Same phase as reactants
- **Heterogeneous**: Different phase; often solid surface
- **Enzyme**: Biological catalyst; highly specific
- **Active Site**: Where substrate binds
- **Lock-and-Key vs Induced Fit**: Models of enzyme-substrate binding

### Reaction Mechanisms
- **Elementary Reaction**: Single molecular event
- **Molecularity**: Unimolecular (A → products), Bimolecular (A + B → products), Termolecular (rare)
- **Intermediate**: Produced then consumed; not in overall equation
- **Rate-Determining Step**: Slowest step controls overall rate
- **Mechanism Validity**: (1) Steps sum to overall equation; (2) Rate law matches experimental

## Equilibrium

### Equilibrium Constants
- **K_c = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b** for aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
- **Pure Solids/Liquids**: Activity = 1; not in K expression
- **K_p = P_C^c P_D^d / P_A^a P_B^b**: For gases
- **K_p = K_c(RT)^Δn**: Δn = Δ(moles gas)
- **K >> 1**: Products favored
- **K << 1**: Reactants favored
- **K ≈ 1**: Significant amounts of both
- **Temperature**: Only thing that changes K

### Reaction Quotient Q
- **Q Same Form as K**: But uses current concentrations, not equilibrium
- **Q < K**: Forward reaction proceeds (shift right)
- **Q > K**: Reverse reaction proceeds (shift left)
- **Q = K**: At equilibrium

### Le Châtelier's Principle
- **Add Reactant/Remove Product**: Shifts right
- **Add Product/Remove Reactant**: Shifts left
- **Increase Pressure (Decrease V)**: Shifts toward fewer gas moles
- **Decrease Pressure (Increase V)**: Shifts toward more gas moles
- **Equal Gas Moles**: Pressure has no effect
- **Add Inert (Const V)**: No effect
- **Increase Temperature**: Shifts in endothermic direction (absorbs heat)
- **Decrease Temperature**: Shifts in exothermic direction (releases heat)
- **Catalyst**: Faster to equilibrium; doesn't shift position or change K

### ICE Tables
- **Initial**: Starting concentrations
- **Change**: Use stoichiometry with variable x
- **Equilibrium**: Initial ± Change
- **Small K Approximation**: If K << 1 and initial large, x << initial
- **5% Rule**: Valid if (x/initial) × 100% < 5%
- **Quadratic**: ax² + bx + c = 0; x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)]/(2a)

### Solubility Equilibrium
- **K_sp = [M^+]^a[X^-]^b** for M_aX_b(s) ⇌ aM^+(aq) + bX^-(aq)
- **Molar Solubility (S)**: Moles dissolved per liter
- **Q_sp < K_sp**: Unsaturated; more dissolves
- **Q_sp = K_sp**: Saturated; equilibrium
- **Q_sp > K_sp**: Supersaturated; precipitation
- **Common Ion Effect**: Decreases solubility
- **pH Effect**: Basic anions more soluble in acid
- **Selective Precipitation**: Use different K_sp to separate ions

## Acids & Bases

### Theories
- **Arrhenius**: Acid gives H⁺, base gives OH⁻ in water
- **Brønsted-Lowry**: Acid donates H⁺, base accepts H⁺
- **Lewis**: Acid accepts e⁻ pair, base donates e⁻ pair
- **Amphoteric**: Can be acid or base (H₂O, HSO₄⁻, amino acids)

### pH and pOH
- **pH = -log[H⁺]**
- **pOH = -log[OH⁻]**
- **pH + pOH = 14** at 25°C
- **K_w = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0×10^-14** at 25°C
- **Neutral**: pH = pOH = 7
- **Acidic**: pH < 7
- **Basic**: pH > 7

### Strong vs Weak
- **Strong Acids**: HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄, HClO₃
- **Strong Bases**: Group 1 hydroxides, heavy Group 2 hydroxides
- **Weak Acids**: Most organic acids, HF, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄
- **Weak Bases**: NH₃, amines
- **K_a**: Acid dissociation constant; larger = stronger
- **K_b**: Base dissociation constant
- **pK_a = -log K_a**
- **pK_b = -log K_b**
- **K_a × K_b = K_w** for conjugate pairs
- **pK_a + pK_b = 14**

### Calculations
- **Strong Acid pH**: pH = -log[H⁺] = -log(Molarity)
- **Strong Base pH**: pOH = -log[OH⁻]; pH = 14 - pOH
- **Weak Acid**: Use K_a and ICE table; often [H⁺] ≈ √(K_a × C_a)
- **Weak Base**: Use K_b and ICE table; [OH⁻] ≈ √(K_b × C_b)
- **Salt Solutions**: Cation from weak base = acidic; Anion from weak acid = basic
- **Polyprotic**: H₃PO₄ has K_a1, K_a2, K_a3; usually only K_a1 matters

### Buffers
- **Composition**: Weak acid + conjugate base OR weak base + conjugate acid
- **Henderson-Hasselbalch**: pH = pK_a + log([A⁻]/[HA])
- **Optimal pH**: pH ≈ pK_a
- **Effective Range**: pK_a ± 1
- **Buffer Capacity**: Maximum when [acid] = [base]
- **Adding Acid**: Weak base neutralizes; ratio changes
- **Adding Base**: Weak acid neutralizes; ratio changes

### Titrations
- **Strong Acid-Strong Base**: Equivalence at pH 7; large jump
- **Weak Acid-Strong Base**: Equivalence pH > 7; buffer region before
- **Strong Acid-Weak Base**: Equivalence pH < 7
- **Half-Equivalence**: pH = pK_a; half neutralized
- **Indicator**: Changes color at specific pH; choose one that changes during equivalence region

## Redox & Electrochemistry - Full Coverage

### Oxidation States
- **Element**: 0
- **Monoatomic Ion**: Charge
- **O**: Usually -2 (peroxide -1, OF₂ +2)
- **H**: +1 with nonmetals, -1 with metals
- **Group 1**: +1; Group 2: +2
- **Sum Rule**: Σ(oxidation states) = charge

### Balancing Redox
- **Half-Reaction Method (Acidic)**:
  1. Separate into oxidation and reduction
  2. Balance atoms except O, H
  3. Balance O with H₂O
  4. Balance H with H⁺
  5. Balance charge with e⁻
  6. Equalize electrons
  7. Add half-reactions
- **Basic Solution**: Do acidic method, then add OH⁻ to neutralize H⁺

### Electrochemical Cells
- **Galvanic**: Spontaneous; produces electricity
- **Electrolytic**: Nonspontaneous; requires electricity
- **Anode**: Oxidation; electrons produced
- **Cathode**: Reduction; electrons consumed
- **Salt Bridge**: Maintains neutrality; allows ion flow
- **Electron Flow**: Anode → cathode (external circuit)
- **Cell Notation**: Anode | Anode soln || Cathode soln | Cathode

### Cell Potential
- **E°_cell = E°_cathode - E°_anode**
- **Positive E°**: Spontaneous (galvanic)
- **Negative E°**: Nonspontaneous (requires power)
- **ΔG° = -nFE°_cell**: F = 96,485 C/mol e⁻
- **E° = (RT/nF) ln K**: At 25°C: E° = (0.0592/n) log K

### Nernst Equation
- **E_cell = E°_cell - (RT/nF) ln Q**
- **At 25°C**: E_cell = E°_cell - (0.0592/n) log Q
- **Concentration Effect**: E_cell changes as reaction proceeds
- **Battery Death**: E_cell → 0 as Q → K

### Electrolysis
- **Faraday's Law**: mol e⁻ = (I × t)/F = Q/F
- **Mass Deposited**: m = (I × t × M)/(n × F)
- **Water Electrolysis**: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
- **Electroplating**: Deposit metal on cathode
- **Aqueous**: May reduce/oxidize water instead of solute


## Organic Chemistry

### Hydrocarbons
- **Alkanes**: C_nH_{2n+2}; sp³; single bonds; saturated
- **Alkenes**: C_nH_{2n}; sp²; C=C double bond; unsaturated
- **Alkynes**: C_nH_{2n-2}; sp; C≡C triple bond
- **Cycloalkanes**: C_nH_{2n}; ring structure
- **Aromatic**: Benzene ring; planar, conjugated, 4n+2 π electrons (Hückel's rule)
- **Aliphatic**: Non-aromatic hydrocarbons
- **Saturated**: No double/triple bonds
- **Unsaturated**: Contains double or triple bonds

### IUPAC Nomenclature
- **Parent Chain**: Longest continuous carbon chain
- **Numbering**: Lowest numbers for substituents/functional groups
- **Substituents**: Alkyl groups (methyl, ethyl, propyl, etc.)
- **Prefixes**: di-, tri-, tetra- for multiple same substituents
- **Alphabetical Order**: Ignore prefixes when alphabetizing
- **Functional Group Priority**: Carboxylic acid > ester > amide > aldehyde > ketone > alcohol > amine > alkene > alkyne > alkane
- **Suffixes**: -ane (alkane), -ene (alkene), -yne (alkyne), -ol (alcohol), -al (aldehyde), -one (ketone), -oic acid (carboxylic)

### Functional Groups
- **Alcohol**: R-OH; -ol suffix; polar, H-bonding
- **Ether**: R-O-R'; ether in name; less reactive than alcohols
- **Aldehyde**: R-CHO; -al suffix; oxidation of 1° alcohol
- **Ketone**: R-CO-R'; -one suffix; oxidation of 2° alcohol
- **Carboxylic Acid**: R-COOH; -oic acid; acidic, H-bonding
- **Ester**: R-COO-R'; -oate; from acid + alcohol; sweet smell
- **Amine**: R-NH₂ (1°), R₂NH (2°), R₃N (3°); basic
- **Amide**: R-CO-NH₂; from acid + amine; peptide bonds
- **Halide**: R-X (X = F, Cl, Br, I); for substitution/elimination
- **Thiol**: R-SH; sulfur analog of alcohol; strong odor

### Reactions
- **Combustion**: Hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
- **Addition**: Alkene/alkyne + reagent → saturated product
  - Hydrogenation: C=C + H₂ → C-C
  - Halogenation: C=C + X₂ → C(-X)-C(-X)
  - Hydrohalogenation: C=C + HX → C(-H)-C(-X); Markovnikov's rule
  - Hydration: C=C + H₂O (H⁺) → C(-H)-C(-OH); Markovnikov
- **Elimination**: Alkyl halide → alkene + HX; requires base
  - E2: Bimolecular; concerted; anti-periplanar
  - E1: Unimolecular; carbocation intermediate; Zaitsev's rule (more substituted alkene favored)
- **Substitution**: R-X + Nu⁻ → R-Nu + X⁻
  - SN1: Unimolecular; carbocation; racemization; 3° > 2° > 1°
  - SN2: Bimolecular; backside attack; inversion; 1° > 2° > 3°
- **Oxidation/Reduction**:
  - 1° alcohol → aldehyde → carboxylic acid
  - 2° alcohol → ketone (can't oxidize further)
  - 3° alcohol: No oxidation
- **Esterification**: Acid + alcohol ⇌ ester + water; acid catalyst
- **Saponification**: Ester + base → carboxylate + alcohol; soap making

### Isomers
- **Constitutional (Structural)**: Different connectivity; different physical/chemical properties
- **Stereoisomers**: Same connectivity, different 3D arrangement
  - **Enantiomers**: Non-superimposable mirror images; chiral center; rotate plane-polarized light
    - R/S Configuration: Cahn-Ingold-Prelog priority rules
    - Optical Activity: (+) dextrorotatory, (-) levorotatory
    - Racemic Mixture: 50:50 enantiomers; no net rotation
  - **Diastereomers**: Not mirror images; multiple chiral centers; different properties
    - Cis/Trans (E/Z): Geometric isomers around double bond or ring
    - Meso Compound: Has chiral centers but achiral due to internal plane of symmetry

### Polymers
- **Addition Polymer**: Monomers add without losing atoms; alkenes
  - Polyethylene: -(CH₂-CH₂)_n-
  - Polypropylene: -(CH₂-CH(CH₃))_n-
  - Polystyrene: -(CH₂-CH(C₆H₅))_n-
  - PVC: -(CH₂-CHCl)_n-
- **Condensation Polymer**: Monomers join with loss of small molecule (H₂O)
  - Polyester: Diol + dicarboxylic acid
  - Polyamide (Nylon): Diamine + dicarboxylic acid; peptide bonds
- **Thermoplastic**: Softens on heating; linear chains
- **Thermoset**: Doesn't soften; cross-linked network

### Biological Molecules
- **Carbohydrates**: (CH₂O)_n; polyhydroxy aldehydes/ketones
  - Monosaccharides: Glucose, fructose, galactose
  - Disaccharides: Sucrose, lactose, maltose
  - Polysaccharides: Starch, cellulose, glycogen
- **Lipids**: Hydrophobic; fats, oils, waxes, steroids
  - Triglycerides: Glycerol + 3 fatty acids
  - Phospholipids: Amphipathic; cell membranes
  - Saturated Fat: No C=C; solid; animal
  - Unsaturated Fat: C=C present; liquid; plant
- **Proteins**: Amino acid polymers; peptide bonds
  - 20 Standard Amino Acids
  - Primary Structure: Sequence
  - Secondary: α-helix, β-sheet (H-bonding)
  - Tertiary: 3D folding (disulfide, ionic, hydrophobic)
  - Quaternary: Multiple polypeptide chains
- **Nucleic Acids**: DNA, RNA; nucleotide polymers
  - Nucleotide: Sugar + phosphate + base
  - Bases: A, T, G, C (DNA); A, U, G, C (RNA)
  - Watson-Crick Base Pairing: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds)

### Spectroscopy
- **Mass Spectrometry (MS)**: Molecular mass; fragmentation pattern; M⁺ peak
- **IR Spectroscopy**: Functional groups; bond vibrations
  - O-H: Broad 3200-3600 cm⁻¹ (alcohol/acid)
  - N-H: 3300-3500 cm⁻¹
  - C=O: Sharp 1650-1750 cm⁻¹ (carbonyl)
  - C=C: 1620-1680 cm⁻¹
  - C≡C: 2100-2260 cm⁻¹
- **¹H NMR**: Hydrogen environments; integration; splitting (n+1 rule)
  - Chemical Shift (δ): Position relative to TMS; ppm
  - Downfield: Deshielded; near electronegative atoms
  - Upfield: Shielded; electron-rich environment
- **¹³C NMR**: Carbon environments; no splitting in proton-decoupled
- **UV-Vis**: Conjugated systems; λ_max; Beer's Law: A = εbc

## Coordination Chemistry

### Complex Ions
- **Central Metal Ion**: Transition metal; accepts electron pairs
- **Ligands**: Lewis bases; donate electron pairs
- **Coordination Number**: Number of ligand attachments (commonly 2, 4, 6)
- **Coordination Sphere**: Metal + ligands in brackets

### Ligand Types
- **Monodentate**: One donor atom (Cl⁻, H₂O, NH₃, CN⁻, OH⁻)
- **Bidentate**: Two donor atoms (en = ethylenediamine, ox = oxalate)
- **Polydentate**: Multiple donor atoms
- **Chelate**: Ring formed by polydentate ligand; extra stable (chelate effect)
- **EDTA**: Hexadentate; six donor atoms; powerful chelating agent
- **Ambidentate**: Can bind through different atoms (SCN⁻: S or N)
- **Bridging**: Connects two metal centers

### Geometries
- **Linear**: CN = 2; 180°
- **Tetrahedral**: CN = 4; 109.5°; often for d⁰ or d¹⁰
- **Square Planar**: CN = 4; 90°; d⁸ (Pt²⁺, Ni²⁺)
- **Octahedral**: CN = 6; 90°; most common for d⁴-d⁷

### Crystal Field Theory (CFT)
- **d-Orbital Splitting**: Ligands cause non-degenerate d-orbitals
- **Octahedral**: e_g (d_{x²-y²}, d_{z²}) higher; t_{2g} (d_{xy}, d_{xz}, d_{yz}) lower
- **Tetrahedral**: Splitting opposite and smaller than octahedral
- **Δ_o or Δ_t**: Crystal field splitting energy
- **Spectrochemical Series**: I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < H₂O < NH₃ < en < NO₂⁻ < CN⁻ < CO (weak → strong field)
- **High Spin**: Weak field; maximum unpaired; small Δ
- **Low Spin**: Strong field; maximum pairing; large Δ
- **d⁴, d⁵, d⁶, d⁷**: Can be high or low spin
- **Color**: d-d transitions; complementary color absorbed

### Nomenclature
- **Cation First**: [complex cation] [anion] or [cation] [complex anion]
- **Ligands Alphabetical**: Ignore prefixes di-, tri-, etc.
- **Prefixes**: di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa- OR bis-, tris-, tetrakis- for complex ligands
- **Ligand Names**: 
  - Anions end in -o: Cl⁻ = chloro, OH⁻ = hydroxo, CN⁻ = cyano
  - Neutral: H₂O = aqua, NH₃ = ammine, CO = carbonyl
- **Metal**: Name + oxidation state (Roman numerals)
- **Anionic Complex**: Metal name ends in -ate
  - Examples: ferrate (Fe), cuprate (Cu), zincate (Zn)

## Nuclear Chemistry

### Radioactivity
- **α (Alpha) Decay**: ⁴₂He nucleus; stopped by paper; ⁴₂α or ⁴₂He²⁺
- **β⁻ (Beta Minus)**: Electron; stopped by Al; neutron → proton + e⁻
- **β⁺ (Positron)**: Positive electron; proton → neutron + e⁺
- **γ (Gamma)**: High-energy photon; no mass/charge; lead shielding
- **Electron Capture**: Nucleus captures inner e⁻; proton → neutron

### Decay Equations
- **Mass Number (A)**: Total nucleons (protons + neutrons)
- **Atomic Number (Z)**: Number of protons
- **α Decay**: A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2
- **β⁻ Decay**: A unchanged, Z increases by 1
- **β⁺ Decay**: A unchanged, Z decreases by 1
- **γ Emission**: A and Z unchanged; energy release only

### Half-Life and Kinetics
- **Half-Life (t₁/₂)**: Time for half to decay
- **First-Order**: N_t = N₀(1/2)^{t/t₁/₂} or N_t = N₀e^{-kt}
- **k = 0.693/t₁/₂**
- **Activity (A)**: Disintegrations per time; Becquerel (Bq) = 1 decay/s; Curie (Ci) = 3.7×10¹⁰ Bq

### Nuclear Reactions
- **Fission**: Heavy nucleus splits; multiple products + neutrons + energy
  - Chain Reaction: Neutrons cause more fissions
  - Critical Mass: Sustains chain reaction
  - Nuclear Reactor: Controlled fission
- **Fusion**: Light nuclei combine; enormous energy; requires extreme T & P
  - Sun: ⁴H → He + energy
  - Deuterium-Tritium: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + energy
- **Transmutation**: Convert one element to another; bombardment with particles

### Binding Energy
- **Mass Defect (Δm)**: Difference between nuclear mass and sum of nucleon masses
- **Binding Energy**: E = Δmc²; energy to disassemble nucleus
- **Binding Energy per Nucleon**: E/A; maximum at Fe-56
- **More Stable**: Higher binding energy per nucleon
- **Fission/Fusion Release Energy**: Products more stable than reactants
