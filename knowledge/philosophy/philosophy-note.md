## 📚 Main Topics

### 1. Logic & Argumentation
The systematic study of valid reasoning.

- **Deductive Reasoning**: Premises guarantee conclusion if valid
  - Syllogism: Major premise → minor premise → conclusion
  - Modus ponens: If P then Q; P; therefore Q
  - Modus tollens: If P then Q; not Q; therefore not P
  - Disjunctive syllogism: P or Q; not P; therefore Q
  - Hypothetical syllogism: If P then Q; if Q then R; therefore if P then R
- **Inductive Reasoning**: Premises make conclusion probable but not certain
  - Generalization: observed instances → general rule
  - Analogy: similar cases → similar conclusion
  - Causal: observed correlation → causal claim
  - Strength depends on sample size, representativeness, counterexamples
- **Validity vs Soundness**:
  - Valid: conclusion follows from premises (structure); can have false premises
  - Sound: valid + all premises are true; guarantees true conclusion
- **Formal Fallacies** (structural errors):
  - Affirming the consequent: If P then Q; Q; therefore P (INVALID)
  - Denying the antecedent: If P then Q; not P; therefore not Q (INVALID)
- **Informal Fallacies** (content/relevance errors):
  - Ad hominem: attack the person, not the argument
  - Straw man: distort opponent's position then attack distortion
  - Appeal to authority: irrelevant authority cited as evidence
  - Appeal to emotion: evoke feelings instead of providing evidence
  - False dilemma: present only two options when more exist
  - Slippery slope: assume chain of extreme consequences without justification
  - Circular reasoning (begging the question): conclusion assumed in premises
  - Red herring: introduce irrelevant topic to divert
  - Equivocation: use word with different meanings in same argument
  - Hasty generalization: insufficient sample for broad conclusion
  - Tu quoque: "you do it too" — doesn't address the argument
  - Bandwagon (ad populum): popular belief ≠ true belief
  - Composition/division: attributing part's properties to whole, or vice versa
- **Propositional Logic**: AND (∧), OR (∨), NOT (¬), IF-THEN (→), IFF (↔)
  - Truth tables; De Morgan's laws; contrapositive
- **Predicate Logic**: Universal (∀) and existential (∃) quantifiers; predicates, variables, domains

### 2. Ethics
The study of morality, right action, and the good life.

- **Metaethics** (nature of morality):
  - Moral realism: objective moral facts exist independent of belief
  - Moral anti-realism: no objective moral facts; includes relativism, error theory, expressivism
  - Moral relativism: morality varies by culture/individual; no universal standards
  - Naturalism: moral facts reducible to natural facts
  - Non-naturalism (Moore): moral properties are sui generis; open question argument
- **Normative Ethics** (how we should act):
  - **Virtue Ethics (Aristotle)**:
    - Character-based; cultivate virtues (excellence of character)
    - Cardinal virtues: prudence, justice, temperance, courage
    - Eudaimonia: human flourishing; the good life through virtuous activity
    - Golden mean: virtue is balance between excess and deficiency
    - MacIntyre: tradition-based; practices and narrative identity
  - **Deontological Ethics (Kant)**:
    - Duty-based; actions evaluated by inherent rightness, not consequences
    - Categorical imperative: act only by rules you could will as universal law
    - Humanity formula: treat people as ends in themselves, never merely as means
    - Good will: only thing good without qualification; moral worth from duty
    - Problems: rigid rules; conflicting duties; ignores consequences
  - **Consequentialism/Utilitarianism**:
    - Actions judged by outcomes; maximize utility (happiness/well-being)
    - Bentham: quantitative; felicific calculus; "greatest good for greatest number"
    - Mill: qualitative; higher/lower pleasures; "better to be Socrates dissatisfied"
    - Act utilitarianism: evaluate each action; rule utilitarianism: follow utility-maximizing rules
    - Problems: justice concerns (sacrificing minority); measuring utility; demandingness
  - **Social Contract**:
    - Hobbes: state of nature is war; sovereign authority for order
    - Locke: natural rights (life, liberty, property); government by consent; right to revolt
    - Rousseau: social contract preserves freedom; general will
    - Rawls: veil of ignorance → principles of justice; difference principle (benefit least advantaged)
  - **Ethics of Care**: Relationships and responsibility central; feminist tradition (Gilligan, Noddings)
  - **Natural Law (Aquinas)**: Moral law derived from nature/reason; accessible through reason; teleological
- **Applied Ethics**: Bioethics (euthanasia, abortion, genetic engineering), environmental ethics, business ethics, animal rights, AI ethics

### 3. Epistemology
The theory of knowledge — its nature, sources, and limits.

- **What is Knowledge?**:
  - Traditional: Justified True Belief (JTB)
  - Gettier problem (1963): JTB is insufficient; accidental truth can satisfy JTB
  - Responses: add "no defeaters" (defeasibility theory); causal theory; reliabilism; virtue epistemology
- **Sources of Knowledge**:
  - **Rationalism** (Descartes, Leibniz, Spinoza): Reason is primary source; innate ideas; a priori knowledge
    - Descartes: methodical doubt → cogito ergo sum ("I think therefore I am")
  - **Empiricism** (Locke, Hume, Berkeley): Experience is primary; tabula rasa; a posteriori knowledge
    - Hume: problem of induction; constant conjunction ≠ necessary connection
    - Berkeley: esse est percipi (to be is to be perceived)
  - **Kant's synthesis**: Space, time, and categories are a priori structures of mind applied to empirical data; synthetic a priori judgments possible
- **Skepticism**:
  - Descartes' evil demon / brain in a vat; external world skepticism
  - Hume: cannot rationally justify induction (problem of induction)
  - Pyrrhonian: suspend judgment on all non-evident matters
  - Responses: foundationalism, coherentism, pragmatism, reliabilism
- **A Priori vs A Posteriori**:
  - A priori: independent of experience (math, logic, "all bachelors are unmarried")
  - A posteriori: dependent on experience ("water boils at 100°C")
- **Analytic vs Synthetic**:
  - Analytic: true by definition ("all bachelors are unmarried")
  - Synthetic: adds new information ("the cat is on the mat")
  - Kant: synthetic a priori exists (mathematics, causation)
  - Quine: challenges analytic-synthetic distinction ("Two Dogmas of Empiricism")
- **Truth Theories**: Correspondence (truth matches reality), coherence (fits within belief system), pragmatic (truth is what works), deflationary ("it is true that P" = "P")

### 4. Metaphysics
The study of fundamental nature of reality.

- **Ontology** (what exists):
  - Materialism/physicalism: only physical matter exists; mind is brain activity
  - Idealism (Berkeley): only minds and ideas exist; physical is perception
  - Dualism (Descartes): mind and body are distinct substances; interaction problem
  - Neutral monism: reality is neither purely mental nor purely physical
  - Abstract objects: do numbers, universals exist? (Platonism vs nominalism)
- **Free Will vs Determinism**:
  - Hard determinism: all events are causally determined; free will is illusion
  - Libertarianism (philosophical): genuine free will exists; agent causation
  - Compatibilism (soft determinism): free will compatible with determinism; freedom = acting on desires without external constraint (Hume, Frankfurt)
- **Personal Identity**: What makes you "you" over time?
  - Memory theory (Locke): psychological continuity through memory
  - Body theory: physical continuity
  - Bundle theory (Hume): no enduring self; just bundle of experiences
  - Narrative identity: self constructed through life story
- **Causation**:
  - Hume: constant conjunction; no necessary connection observable
  - Counterfactual: A caused B if B wouldn't have occurred without A (Lewis)
  - Regularity theory, probabilistic causation, manipulationist theories
- **Time**: A-theory (past/present/future are real distinctions) vs B-theory (all times equally real; tenseless); presentism vs eternalism; McTaggart's paradox
- **Possible Worlds**: Modal logic; necessity and possibility; Kripke, Lewis (modal realism)

### 5. Political Philosophy
Justice, rights, governance, and legitimate authority.

- **Classical Liberalism**: Individual rights; limited government; rule of law; consent of governed
  - Locke: natural rights (life, liberty, property); social contract; revolution justified against tyranny
  - Mill: harm principle — liberty limited only to prevent harm to others; freedom of speech
- **Justice as Fairness (Rawls)**:
  - Original position and veil of ignorance: choose principles without knowing your place in society
  - Two principles: (1) equal basic liberties; (2) social/economic inequalities only if they benefit the least advantaged (difference principle) and attached to offices open to all
- **Libertarianism (Nozick)**: Minimal state; property rights paramount; entitlement theory; any distribution just if arrived at through just transfers; Wilt Chamberlain argument against redistribution
- **Communitarianism**: Critique of liberal individualism; shared values and community bonds matter (MacIntyre, Sandel, Taylor, Walzer)
- **Marxism**: Class struggle; exploitation of labor; surplus value; alienation; historical materialism; means of production; proletariat vs bourgeoisie; communist society = classless
- **Feminism**: Wollstonecraft, de Beauvoir, hooks; patriarchy critique; intersectionality; ethics of care
- **Critical Theory (Frankfurt School)**: Critique of ideology; domination; Habermas — communicative rationality, public sphere
- **Anarchism**: No legitimate state authority; voluntary cooperation; Kropotkin, Bakunin
- **Democracy**: Direct vs representative; deliberative democracy; tyranny of the majority; democratic legitimacy

### 6. Philosophy of Mind
The nature of consciousness, mental states, and the mind-body problem.

- **Mind-Body Problem**: How do mental and physical relate?
  - Substance dualism (Descartes): two distinct substances; interaction problem (pineal gland?)
  - Property dualism: one substance, both physical and mental properties
  - Behaviorism: mental states = behavioral dispositions (Ryle); critiqued (Chisholm, Putnam)
  - Identity theory: mental states = brain states (Place, Smart); type vs token identity
  - Functionalism: mental states defined by functional role (inputs→state→outputs); Turing; multiple realizability
  - Eliminativism: folk psychology concepts (belief, desire) will be eliminated by neuroscience (Churchlands)
- **Consciousness**: Hard problem (Chalmers) — why is there subjective experience at all?
  - Qualia: subjective quality of experience ("what it's like"); Mary's Room thought experiment (Jackson)
  - Chinese Room (Searle): syntax ≠ semantics; computers don't truly "understand"
  - Zombie argument: conceivable being physically identical but without consciousness
  - Integrated Information Theory (IIT), Global Workspace Theory; neuroscience of consciousness
- **Intentionality**: "Aboutness" of mental states; beliefs are about something; Brentano; how does brain produce meaningful content?
- **Free Will & Agency**: How it connects to mental causation; epiphenomenalism (mental has no causal power); Libet experiments

### 7. Aesthetics
Philosophy of art, beauty, and taste.

- **What is Art?**: Representation theory, expression theory, institutional theory (Dickie), cluster concept
- **Aesthetic Judgment**: Kant — disinterested pleasure; universal but subjective; beauty vs sublime
- **Taste**: Is it objective or subjective? Hume: standard of taste through qualified judges; Kant: sensus communis
- **Value of Art**: Intrinsic vs instrumental; art for art's sake vs art with social function
- **Philosophy of Music, Film, Literature**: Medium-specific questions; emotional response to fiction; paradox of fiction

### 8. History of Philosophy
Major periods, movements, and thinkers.

- **Ancient** (600 BCE - 500 CE):
  - Pre-Socratics: Thales (water), Heraclitus (change), Parmenides (permanence), Democritus (atoms)
  - Socrates: Socratic method; examined life; know thyself; virtue is knowledge
  - Plato: Theory of Forms; Allegory of the Cave; philosopher-kings; divided line; tripartite soul
  - Aristotle: empiricism; four causes (material, formal, efficient, final); virtue ethics; logic; categories
  - Hellenistic: Epicureanism (pleasure/ataraxia), Stoicism (virtue/apatheia), Skepticism (suspension)
- **Medieval** (500-1500):
  - Augustine: faith + reason; problem of evil; free will; City of God
  - Aquinas: five ways (proofs of God); natural law; faith and reason harmonize
  - Ockham's Razor: do not multiply entities beyond necessity
- **Modern** (1600-1900):
  - Descartes: cogito; methodical doubt; mind-body dualism; rationalism
  - Locke: tabula rasa; empiricism; social contract; natural rights
  - Hume: skepticism; problem of induction; is-ought gap; bundle theory of self
  - Kant: synthetic a priori; categorical imperative; transcendental idealism; Critique of Pure Reason
  - Hegel: dialectic (thesis-antithesis-synthesis); absolute idealism; history as rational progress
  - Mill: utilitarianism; liberty; harm principle; on the subjection of women
  - Kierkegaard: existentialism; anxiety; leap of faith; three stages (aesthetic, ethical, religious)
  - Nietzsche: death of God; will to power; eternal recurrence; übermensch; critique of morality
  - Marx: historical materialism; class struggle; alienation; communist manifesto
- **Contemporary** (1900-present):
  - Analytic: Russell, Wittgenstein (Tractatus → Investigations), logical positivism; Quine; Kripke; ordinary language
  - Continental: Husserl (phenomenology), Heidegger (being-in-the-world, dasein), Sartre (existence precedes essence), Merleau-Ponty (embodied perception)
  - Existentialism: Sartre (radical freedom, bad faith), Camus (absurd), de Beauvoir (feminist existentialism)
  - Pragmatism: James, Dewey, Peirce; truth as what works; anti-foundationalism
  - Postmodernism: Foucault (power/knowledge), Derrida (deconstruction), Lyotard (end of metanarratives)
  - Rawls (justice), Nozick (libertarianism), Singer (animal rights), Parfit (personal identity, reasons)
