## 📚 Main Topics

### 1. Ancient Civilizations
The earliest complex societies with writing, cities, and organized government.

- **Mesopotamia** (c. 3500-539 BCE, modern Iraq):
  - Sumer: first civilization; cuneiform writing; city-states (Ur, Uruk); ziggurats
  - Akkadian Empire (Sargon ~2334 BCE): first major empire
  - Babylonia: Hammurabi's Code (~1754 BCE) — earliest known written legal code; eye-for-an-eye principle
  - Assyria: military empire; iron weapons; library of Ashurbanipal
  - Neo-Babylonia: Nebuchadnezzar II; Hanging Gardens; fell to Persia (539 BCE)
  - Innovations: wheel, plow, irrigation, 60-base number system (hours/minutes), astronomy
- **Ancient Egypt** (c. 3100-30 BCE):
  - Nile floods enabled agriculture; "gift of the Nile"
  - Pharaohs: god-kings; Old Kingdom (pyramids at Giza, Sphinx), Middle Kingdom (expansion), New Kingdom (Hatshepsut, Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, Ramesses II)
  - Hieroglyphics; Rosetta Stone (Champollion 1822) unlocked translation
  - Afterlife beliefs: mummification, Book of the Dead, tombs (Valley of the Kings)
  - Innovations: papyrus, calendar (365 days), advanced medicine, monumental architecture
- **Indus Valley** (c. 2600-1900 BCE, modern Pakistan/India):
  - Harappa and Mohenjo-daro: planned grid cities, drainage systems, standardized weights
  - Undeciphered script; likely trade with Mesopotamia
  - Decline: possibly climate change, river shifts, or invasion
- **Ancient China** (c. 2000 BCE onward):
  - Shang Dynasty: bronze casting, oracle bones (earliest Chinese writing)
  - Zhou Dynasty: Mandate of Heaven (legitimacy of rulers); feudalism; Confucius, Laozi
  - Spring and Autumn / Warring States periods: philosophical flourishing (Hundred Schools)

### 2. Classical Antiquity
Greece and Rome — foundations of Western civilization.

- **Ancient Greece** (c. 800-146 BCE):
  - City-states (polis): Athens (democracy), Sparta (military state), Corinth, Thebes
  - **Athenian Democracy**: Direct democracy for male citizens; Assembly (ekklesia), Council (boule), law courts; Cleisthenes' reforms (508 BCE); Pericles' golden age
  - Persian Wars (490-479 BCE): Marathon, Thermopylae (300 Spartans), Salamis → Greek victory; united city-states
  - Peloponnesian War (431-404 BCE): Athens vs Sparta → Spartan victory; weakened all Greeks
  - Philosophy: Socrates (dialectical method, examined life), Plato (Forms, Republic, Academy), Aristotle (empiricism, logic, Lyceum, tutor of Alexander)
  - Culture: tragedy (Sophocles, Euripides), comedy (Aristophanes); Herodotus/Thucydides (history); Homer (Iliad, Odyssey); Olympic Games (776 BCE onward)
  - Alexander the Great (356-323 BCE): Macedonian; conquered Persian Empire, Egypt, to India; Hellenistic spread of Greek culture
- **Roman Republic & Empire** (c. 509 BCE - 476 CE):
  - Republic: Senate + consuls; Twelve Tables (written law); patricians vs plebeians; Tribune of the plebs
  - Punic Wars (264-146 BCE): Rome vs Carthage; Hannibal crossed Alps; destruction of Carthage → Roman Mediterranean dominance
  - Julius Caesar: conquered Gaul; crossed Rubicon (49 BCE); dictator; assassinated 44 BCE (Ides of March)
  - Augustus (27 BCE - 14 CE): first emperor; Pax Romana (~200 years of peace and prosperity)
  - Roman innovations: roads, aqueducts, concrete, arches, legal system (basis of civil law), Latin language
  - Christianity: from Jewish sect to state religion (Constantine, Edict of Milan 313 CE; Theodosius 380 CE)
  - Decline and fall: overexpansion, barbarian invasions, internal corruption, economic troubles, division (East/West 395 CE), fall of Western Roman Empire (476 CE)

### 3. Medieval Period
The "Middle Ages" between classical antiquity and the modern era.

- **Byzantine Empire** (330-1453):
  - Eastern Roman Empire; capital Constantinople; preserved Greek/Roman knowledge; Justinian's Code
  - Orthodox Christianity; Hagia Sophia; iconoclasm controversy
  - Fell to Ottoman Turks (1453); scholars fled to Italy → contributed to Renaissance
- **Islamic Golden Age** (c. 750-1258):
  - Abbasid Caliphate; Baghdad (House of Wisdom); preservation/translation of Greek texts
  - Mathematics: algebra (al-Khwarizmi), algorithm, Arabic numerals (from India)
  - Science: optics (Ibn al-Haytham), medicine (Ibn Sina/Avicenna), astronomy
  - Trade networks: Silk Road, Indian Ocean; paper from China; spread knowledge
- **Feudalism** (c. 800-1400, Europe):
  - Political: king → lords → vassals → serfs; land (fiefs) in exchange for military service
  - Manorialism: economic system; self-sufficient estates; serfs tied to land
  - Catholic Church: dominant institution; Pope, bishops, monasteries; moral authority
  - Charlemagne (800 CE): crowned Holy Roman Emperor; unified much of Western Europe
- **Key Events**:
  - Magna Carta (1215): King John forced to accept limits on royal power; foundation for constitutional law; due process, rule of law
  - Crusades (1095-1291): Christian military campaigns to Holy Land; cultural exchange; increased trade; weakened feudalism
  - Black Death (1347-1351): Bubonic plague; killed ~30-60% of European population; labor shortage → higher wages; social upheaval; questioned Church authority
  - Hundred Years War (1337-1453): England vs France; Joan of Arc (1429); longbow; gunpowder; rise of national identity
  - Mongol Empire (1206-1368): Genghis Khan; largest contiguous land empire; Pax Mongolica enabled trade; devastated Central Asia/Middle East

### 4. Renaissance & Early Modern
Cultural rebirth and the seeds of the modern world.

- **Renaissance** (c. 1350-1600, Italy → Europe):
  - Humanism: focus on human potential, classical learning, secular thinking
  - Art: perspective, naturalism; da Vinci (Mona Lisa, Vitruvian Man), Michelangelo (Sistine Chapel, David), Raphael, Botticelli
  - Literature: Petrarch (father of humanism), Boccaccio, Machiavelli (The Prince — realist politics)
  - Architecture: Brunelleschi (Florence dome); revival of classical forms
  - Printing press (Gutenberg, c. 1440): mass production of books; spread literacy and ideas; crucial for Reformation and Scientific Revolution
- **Reformation** (1517 onward):
  - Martin Luther: 95 Theses (1517); criticized indulgences; sola scriptura, sola fide
  - Protestant denominations: Lutheran, Calvinist (predestination), Anglican (Henry VIII's break with Rome)
  - Counter-Reformation: Council of Trent (1545-1563); Jesuits (Ignatius of Loyola); Inquisition
  - Religious wars: Thirty Years War (1618-1648); Peace of Westphalia → sovereign states principle
- **Scientific Revolution** (c. 1543-1687):
  - Copernicus: heliocentric model (De Revolutionibus, 1543); challenged geocentric Church doctrine
  - Galileo: telescope observations (moons of Jupiter); support for Copernicus; persecution by Church
  - Kepler: laws of planetary motion (elliptical orbits)
  - Newton: Principia (1687); laws of motion and universal gravitation; calculus; "standing on the shoulders of giants"
  - Francis Bacon: empirical method; inductive reasoning
  - Descartes: rationalism; "I think therefore I am"; coordinate geometry
- **Age of Exploration** (c. 1415-1600):
  - Motives: God, gold, glory; spice trade; new routes to Asia
  - Portugal: Henry the Navigator; Dias (Cape of Good Hope); Vasco da Gama (India)
  - Spain: Columbus (1492, Americas); Magellan's expedition (first circumnavigation)
  - Columbian Exchange: transfer of plants (potatoes, tomatoes, corn, tobacco), animals (horses, cattle), diseases (smallpox devastated indigenous peoples)
  - Colonial empires: Spanish, Portuguese, Dutch, British, French
- **Enlightenment** (c. 1685-1815):
  - Reason, individual rights, scientific method applied to society
  - Locke: natural rights (life, liberty, property); government by consent; tabula rasa
  - Voltaire: free speech, religious tolerance; criticism of Catholic Church
  - Montesquieu: separation of powers (legislative, executive, judicial)
  - Rousseau: social contract; general will; noble savage; education reform
  - Adam Smith: Wealth of Nations (1776); free market, invisible hand, division of labor
  - Kant: "dare to know" (sapere aude); categorical imperative; Critique of Pure Reason
  - Influenced: American Revolution, French Revolution, constitutionalism, human rights

### 5. Age of Revolutions
Political and industrial transformation.

- **American Revolution** (1775-1783):
  - "No taxation without representation"; Boston Tea Party (1773)
  - Declaration of Independence (1776, Jefferson): natural rights, consent of governed
  - Key battles: Lexington/Concord, Saratoga (French alliance), Yorktown (British surrender)
  - Constitution (1787): federalism, separation of powers, checks and balances
  - Bill of Rights (1791): individual liberties (speech, religion, press, assembly)
- **French Revolution** (1789-1799):
  - Causes: fiscal crisis, inequality (Three Estates), Enlightenment ideas, American example
  - Storming of Bastille (July 14, 1789); Declaration of the Rights of Man
  - Phases: constitutional monarchy → republic → Reign of Terror (Robespierre, guillotine) → Directory → Napoleon
  - Napoleon (1799-1815): coup d'état; Napoleonic Code (civil law); continental system; conquered much of Europe; defeated at Waterloo (1815)
  - Legacy: spread of liberal ideas, nationalism, end of feudalism in many areas
- **Industrial Revolution** (c. 1760-1840, Britain → world):
  - Agricultural revolution: enclosure movement, crop rotation → food surplus → labor for factories
  - Key inventions: steam engine (Watt), spinning jenny, power loom, cotton gin
  - Factory system: mass production; division of labor; urbanization
  - Social effects: child labor, poor working conditions, rise of middle class, labor unions
  - Transport: railways (Stephenson), steamships, canals → connected markets
  - Second Industrial Revolution (late 1800s): steel, electricity, chemicals, internal combustion engine, telegraph/telephone
- **Other Revolutions**: Latin American independence (Bolívar, San Martín); Haitian Revolution (1791-1804, Toussaint); 1848 revolutions across Europe

### 6. World Wars & 20th Century
The most destructive conflicts in human history.

- **World War I** (1914-1918):
  - Causes (MAIN): Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism; spark: assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (Sarajevo, 1914)
  - Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Ottoman) vs Triple Entente (Britain, France, Russia; later US, Italy, Japan)
  - Trench warfare: Western Front (France/Belgium); massive casualties; stalemate
  - New technology: machine guns, poison gas, tanks, aircraft, submarines
  - Key events: Gallipoli, Verdun, Somme; US entry (1917); Russian Revolution (1917)
  - End: Armistice (Nov 11, 1918); Treaty of Versailles (1919) — war guilt clause, reparations, territorial losses, League of Nations
  - Consequences: empires dissolved (Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, German); redrew Middle East (Sykes-Picot); set stage for WWII
- **Interwar Period** (1919-1939):
  - Roaring Twenties → Great Depression (1929): stock market crash, bank failures, unemployment
  - Rise of totalitarianism: Fascism (Mussolini 1922, Italy); Nazism (Hitler 1933, Germany); Stalinism (USSR)
  - Appeasement: Munich Agreement (1938); Chamberlain "peace in our time"
- **World War II** (1939-1945):
  - Axis (Germany, Italy, Japan) vs Allies (Britain, France, USSR, US, China)
  - European theater: Blitzkrieg; Fall of France (1940); Battle of Britain; Operation Barbarossa (invasion of USSR); Stalingrad (turning point); D-Day (June 6, 1944); fall of Berlin (1945)
  - Pacific theater: Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941); Midway; island hopping; atomic bombs (Hiroshima Aug 6, Nagasaki Aug 9, 1945)
  - Holocaust: Nazi genocide of 6 million Jews; also targeted Roma, disabled, homosexuals, political prisoners
  - Consequences: ~70-85 million dead; UN founded (1945); Nuremberg Trials; beginning of Cold War; decolonization; US and USSR as superpowers

### 7. Cold War & Decolonization
Bipolar world order and the end of empires.

- **Cold War** (1947-1991):
  - US (capitalism, democracy) vs USSR (communism, one-party state); ideological, economic, military competition
  - Not direct warfare — proxy wars, espionage, propaganda, arms race
  - Nuclear arms race: MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction); Cuban Missile Crisis (1962, closest to nuclear war)
  - Key events: Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan, NATO (1949) vs Warsaw Pact (1955); Korean War (1950-53); Vietnam War (1955-75); Berlin Wall (1961-1989)
  - Space Race: Sputnik (1957), Gagarin (1961), moon landing (Apollo 11, 1969)
  - Détente (1970s): arms control (SALT); Helsinki Accords
  - End: Gorbachev (glasnost/perestroika); fall of Berlin Wall (1989); dissolution of USSR (1991)
- **Decolonization** (1945-1970s):
  - India: Gandhi (nonviolent resistance, satyagraha); independence + partition (1947); Hindu-Muslim violence
  - Africa: Pan-Africanism; Ghana (Nkrumah, 1957); Kenya (Mau Mau); South Africa (apartheid → Mandela, 1994)
  - Middle East: Israel (1948); Arab-Israeli conflicts; Suez Crisis (1956); oil politics (OPEC)
  - Southeast Asia: Indonesia (Sukarno); Vietnam (Ho Chi Minh); Cambodia (Khmer Rouge)
  - Legacy: new nations, border disputes, neocolonialism, development challenges

### 8. Asian History
Major civilizations and modern transformations.

- **Chinese Dynasties**:
  - Qin (221-206 BCE): unification; Shi Huangdi; Great Wall; standardized writing/weights; Legalism; terracotta army
  - Han (206 BCE-220 CE): Silk Road trade; Confucianism as state ideology; paper invention; expansion
  - Tang (618-907): golden age; poetry (Li Bai, Du Fu); cosmopolitan; printing; meritocratic exams
  - Song (960-1279): inventions (gunpowder, compass, movable type printing); economic growth; Neo-Confucianism
  - Yuan (1271-1368): Mongol rule (Kublai Khan); Marco Polo visited; cultural exchange
  - Ming (1368-1644): Great Wall reconstruction; Zheng He's voyages; porcelain; Forbidden City
  - Qing (1644-1912): last dynasty; Manchu; Opium Wars (1839-42, 1856-60); unequal treaties; Taiping Rebellion; Boxer Rebellion; fall → Republic of China (1912)
  - Modern: Mao Zedong (PRC 1949); Great Leap Forward; Cultural Revolution; Deng Xiaoping reforms (1978); economic transformation
- **Japanese History**:
  - Feudal: samurai, daimyo, shogunate (Tokugawa 1603-1868); isolationism (sakoku)
  - Meiji Restoration (1868): rapid modernization; industrialization; Western-style constitution
  - Imperial expansion: Sino-Japanese War (1894-95), Russo-Japanese War (1904-05), Korea annexation (1910)
  - WWII: Pearl Harbor; atomic bombs; occupation; pacifist constitution (Article 9)
  - Post-war: economic miracle (1950s-1980s); technology leader; demographic challenges
- **Korean History**:
  - Three Kingdoms (57 BCE-668 CE): Goguryeo, Baekje, Silla
  - Unified Silla (668-935); Goryeo Dynasty (935-1392): celadon pottery; Buddhism; Mongol invasions; name "Korea" derived
  - Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897): Confucian state; King Sejong (hangul alphabet, 1443); civil service exams; Japanese invasions (Imjin War 1592-98)
  - Japanese occupation (1910-1945): cultural suppression; forced labor; comfort women
  - Korean War (1950-1953): North (communist, Kim Il-sung, backed by China/USSR) vs South (capitalist, backed by US/UN); armistice (DMZ, 38th parallel); still technically at war
  - South Korea: Miracle on the Han River; democratization (1987); K-pop, technology, cultural exports (hallyu)
  - North Korea: Kim dynasty; nuclear program; isolation; human rights concerns
- **Indian History**:
  - Maurya Empire (322-185 BCE): Ashoka (spread Buddhism; non-violence)
  - Gupta Empire (320-550 CE): golden age; mathematics (zero, decimal), literature, art
  - Mughal Empire (1526-1857): Akbar (religious tolerance), Shah Jahan (Taj Mahal); Hindu-Muslim relations
  - British colonial rule (1757-1947): British East India Company → British Raj; railways, English education; exploitation; famines
  - Independence movement: Gandhi (non-violent resistance, salt march 1930); Nehru; partition (India/Pakistan, 1947); communal violence
  - Modern India: world's largest democracy; economic liberalization (1991); nuclear capability; IT industry

### 9. Contemporary World
Major trends and events since 1991.

- **Globalization**: Trade, finance, culture, technology increasingly interconnected; WTO; multinational corporations
- **European Integration**: EU (Maastricht Treaty 1992); euro (1999); enlargement; Brexit (2020)
- **War on Terror**: 9/11 (2001); Afghanistan War (2001-2021); Iraq War (2003-2011); ISIS
- **Rise of China**: Economic superpower; Belt and Road; South China Sea; US-China competition
- **Digital Revolution**: Internet, smartphones, social media; AI and automation; data privacy
- **Climate Change**: Paris Agreement (2015); extreme weather; renewable energy; sustainability
- **Challenges**: Global pandemics (COVID-19); populism; migration; inequality; democratic backsliding; nuclear proliferation

## 🔗 Interconnections
- Ancient civilizations created foundations (writing, law, agriculture) for all subsequent developments
- Philosophy and religion shaped political structures across all eras
- Technology (printing, steam, computing) drove revolutionary change
- Colonialism and its legacy connect European, Asian, African, and American histories
- Economic systems (feudalism → mercantilism → capitalism → globalization) evolved across eras
